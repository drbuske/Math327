    Handout Daily Prep 4.1 - Vectors, Mappings, and Matrices
    

    Section Overview

    In this section we will learn how vectors, linear mappings, and matrices provide a systematic way to organize and solve problems involving many variables. The text motivates linear algebra by showing how even simple systems of equations generalize to high‑dimensional settings, where matrices and vector operations become essential tools. You will explore n‑dimensional space, interpret vectors as geometric arrows, and see how linear equations, functions, and differential‑equation models all benefit from a unified matrix‑based framework.
    🔗
    🔗

    These are the tasks you should be able to perform with reasonable fluency when you arrive at our next class meeting. Important new vocabulary words are indicated in italics.
    🔗

      🔗
      🔗
    • Recognize how systems of linear equations can be organized and solved more systematically using matrices.
      🔗

    • Interpret basic vector operations such as addition and scalar multiplication.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Meaning of a Vector.
    An expression like \(\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix}\) represents:
    🔗
    • A point or vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)
    • Correct. An \(n\)-tuple as a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)
    • A single real number.
    • No—this notation represents multiple components.
    • A 2‑dimensional point only.
    • No—it generalizes to any dimension.
    🔗
    2. Adding Vectors.
    Let \(\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\\3\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}4\\0\\-1\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Compute \(\vec{u}+\vec{v}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}5\\2\\2\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Correct. Vectors are added component‑wise.
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}5\\2\\-1\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Incorrect third component.
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}3\\2\\4\end{bmatrix}\)
    • No—this is not componentwise addition.
    🔗
    3. Scalar Multiplication.
    Compute \(3\vec{w}\) where \(\vec{w}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\-1\\4\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}6\\-3\\12\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Correct. Multiply each component by the scalar.
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}3\\-1\\4\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Only the first component was scaled.
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}6\\2\\8\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Incorrect scaling of the second and third components.
    🔗
    4. Definition of a Linear Mapping.
    A mapping \(T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m\) is linear if:
    🔗
    • \(T(\vec{u}+\vec{v}) = T(\vec{u}) + T(\vec{v})\) and \(T(c\vec{u}) = c\,T(\vec{u})\text{.}\)
    • Correct. This is the definition of linearity.
    • T must preserve vector lengths.
    • No—only orthogonal mappings do that.
    • T must output a scalar.
    • No—it outputs vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    5. Columns of a Matrix.
    For a linear mapping \(T(\vec{x})=A\vec{x}\text{,}\) what does each column of \(A\) represent?
    🔗
    • The vector \(T(\vec{e}_i)\text{,}\) the image of the \(i\)-th standard basis vector.
    • Correct. Columns of \(A\) are \(T(\vec{e}_1),\dots,T(\vec{e}_n)\text{.}\)
    • The eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)
    • No—eigenvalues are not arranged as columns.
    • Randomly chosen vectors.
    • No—each column encodes \(T\) fully.
    🔗
    6. Matrix–Vector Multiplication.
    Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 2\\3 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\vec{x}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\4\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Compute \(A\vec{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}9\\7\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Correct: \(1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot 4 = 9\text{,}\) \(3\cdot 1 + 1\cdot 4 = 7\text{.}\)
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix}\)
    • No—these are row sums, not \(A\vec{x}\text{.}\)
    • \(\begin{bmatrix}1\\4\end{bmatrix}\)
    • No—that is just \(\vec{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    7. Writing a System as \(A\vec{x}=\vec{b}\).
    The system \(x + y = 2\text{,}\) \(2x + y = 4\) can be written as \(A\vec{x}=\vec{b}\text{.}\) What is \(A\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(A=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 1\\2 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\)
    • Correct. The rows contain the coefficients of the system.
    • \(A=\begin{bmatrix}2 \amp 4\\1 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\)
    • No—the coefficients are incorrect.
    • \(A=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 0\\0 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\)
    • No—that would yield the trivial system \(x=\vec{b}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    8. Dimension of \(\mathbb{R}^n\).
    What is the dimension of \(\mathbb{R}^5\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • 5
    • Correct. \(\mathbb{R}^n\) consists of all \(n\)-tuples of real numbers.
    • 4
    • No—there are five components.
    • 10
    • No—this might be confused with combinations; not relevant here.
    🔗
    9.
    In your own words, describe what a linear operator is.
    🔗
    Solution.
    A linear operator is a function that takes a vector as input and outputs another vector, while preserving the two key properties of linearity:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L(\vec{u}+\vec{v}) = L(\vec{u}) + L(\vec{v}), \qquad L(c\vec{u}) = c\,L(\vec{u}), \end{equation*}
    for all vectors \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\) and scalars \(c\text{.}\) If the operator maps a vector space to itself (for example, \(L:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n\)), then it is called a linear operator rather than just a linear mapping. In finite dimensions, these are exactly the operations that can be represented by matrices.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    Give another word for the magnitude of a vector.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Another common word for the magnitude of a vector is its length. In linear algebra, magnitude is also called the norm of the vector.

    11.
    In the text, the author writes the (incorrect) statement: “The reason these are called a basis is that every other vector can be written as a linear combination of them.” Explain precisely what is wrong with this statement, using the correct definitions of basis, span, and linear independence.
    🔗
    Solution.
    What the author wrote is only half of the definition of a basis. For a set of vectors \(\{ \vec{v}_1,\dots,\vec{v}_k\}\) to be a basis for a vector space, two things must be true:
    🔗
    1. Span: Every vector in the space can be written as a linear combination of \(\vec{v}_1,\dots,\vec{v}_k\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Linear independence: No nontrivial linear combination of the vectors equals the zero vector.
      🔗
      🔗
    The author’s sentence mentions only the spanning property, but not the requirement of linear independence. Without linear independence, a spanning set is just a spanning set, not a basis. For example, in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{,}\) the vectors
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \vec{u}_1=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix},\qquad \vec{u}_2=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix},\qquad \vec{u}_3=\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    span \(\mathbb{R}^2\) but are not linearly independent, so they do not form a basis. A correct definition requires both spanning and linear independence.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    12.
    The textbook says: “Hence, we may think of matrices being linear mappings, and linear mappings being matrices.” Then it immediately warns this is not always true. Using the linear operator \(L(f)=f'\) on smooth functions, explain why a general linear mapping is not always representable by a finite matrix. Extra credit: Describe an infinite-dimensional “matrix” that represents \(L\) using Taylor series ideas.
    🔗
    Solution.
    On a finite-dimensional vector space such as \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) every linear mapping can be represented by some \(n\times n\) matrix. But the mapping \(L(f)=f'\) acts on a space of smooth functions, which is infinite-dimensional. There is no finite list of basis vectors, so there is no finite matrix that could represent \(L\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Extra credit idea: If a smooth function \(f\) is written as a Taylor series
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k t^k, \end{equation*}
    then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(t)=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k\,a_k\,t^{\,k-1}. \end{equation*}
    In the basis \(\{1, t, t^2, t^3,\dots\}\text{,}\) differentiation is represented by an infinite matrix:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L \ \leftrightarrow \ \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \cdots\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp \cdots\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp \cdots\\ \vdots \amp \vdots \amp \vdots \amp \vdots \amp \ddots \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    This “matrix” is infinite in extent, reflecting the fact that the underlying vector space is infinite-dimensional. That is why not all linear mappings are representable by finite matrices.
    🔗
    🔗

    13.
    Label the vectors \(\vec{u}= \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) \(\vec{v}= \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{u}+\vec{v}\) in Figure 19.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Computation. We have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}, \qquad \vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad \vec{u}+\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}1+2\\3+1\end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    The sum \(\vec{u}+\vec{v}\) is drawn from the origin to \((3,4)\text{.}\) Figure 20 shows the labels placed along each arrow.
    🔗
    Thus the required labels are:
    • \(\vec{u}\) along the arrow to \((1,3)\text{,}\)
      🔗
    • \(\vec{v}\) along the arrow to \((2,1)\text{,}\) and
      🔗
    • \(\vec{u}+\vec{v}\) along the arrow to \((3,4)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    The faint segments complete the parallelogram rule construction.

    14.
    Let \(\displaystyle \vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Express (draw) \(\vec{u}\text{,}\) \(2\vec{u}\text{,}\) and \(-\tfrac{3}{2}\vec{u}\) on the graph in Figure 21.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Compute the scaled vectors:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}, \qquad 2\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\4\end{bmatrix}, \qquad -\tfrac{3}{2}\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}-\tfrac{3}{2}\\-3\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    The figure below places arrows from the origin to the endpoints \((1,2)\text{,}\) \((2,4)\text{,}\) and \(\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2},-3\bigr)\text{,}\) and labels each vector.
    🔗
    Hence the three vectors are correctly drawn to the points \((1,2)\text{,}\) \((2,4)\text{,}\) and \(\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2},-3\bigr)\text{,}\) respectively.

    15.
    Let \(\displaystyle \vec{v}_{1}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\displaystyle \vec{v}_{2}=\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Express each of the following vectors as a linear combination of \(\vec{v}_{1}\) and \(\vec{v}_{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \vec{a}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\3\end{bmatrix}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \vec{b}=\begin{bmatrix}-4\\1\end{bmatrix}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle \vec{c}=\begin{bmatrix}6\\6\end{bmatrix}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    We solve for scalars \(\alpha,\beta\) such that:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \alpha\vec{v}_{1}+\beta\vec{v}_{2} = \alpha\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix} +\beta\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2\alpha - 2\beta \\ \alpha + 2\beta\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    1) For \(\vec{a}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\3\end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2\alpha - 2\beta = 0,\qquad \alpha + 2\beta = 3. \end{equation*}
    From the first equation \(\alpha=\beta\text{.}\) Substitute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \alpha + 2\alpha = 3 \Rightarrow 3\alpha = 3 \Rightarrow \alpha = 1. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\beta=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\vec{a} = \vec{v}_{1} + \vec{v}_{2}}. \end{equation*}
    2) For \(\vec{b}=\begin{bmatrix}-4\\1\end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2\alpha - 2\beta = -4,\qquad \alpha + 2\beta = 1. \end{equation*}
    Divide first eqn by 2: \(\alpha - \beta = -2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \alpha - \beta = -2,\qquad \alpha + 2\beta = 1. \end{equation*}
    Subtract the first from the second: \(3\beta = 3\) so \(\beta=1\text{.}\) Then \(\alpha - 1 = -2\text{,}\) so \(\alpha=-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\vec{b} = -\vec{v}_{1} + \vec{v}_{2}}. \end{equation*}
    3) For \(\vec{c}=\begin{bmatrix}6\\6\end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2\alpha - 2\beta = 6,\qquad \alpha + 2\beta = 6. \end{equation*}
    Divide first eqn: \(\alpha - \beta = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \alpha - \beta = 3,\qquad \alpha + 2\beta = 6. \end{equation*}
    Subtract: \(3\beta = 3\) → \(\beta=1\text{.}\) Then \(\alpha - 1 = 3\) → \(\alpha=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\vec{c} = 4\vec{v}_{1} + \vec{v}_{2}}. \end{equation*}

    16.
    Suppose a linear mapping \(F:\mathbb{R}^{2}\to\mathbb{R}^{3}\) takes \((1,0)\) to \((1,2,3)\) and takes \((0,1)\) to \((4,5,6)\text{.}\) Write down the matrix representing \(F\) (with respect to the standard bases).
    🔗
    Solution.
    In the standard bases, the matrix of a linear map has as its columns the images of the standard basis vectors \(\vec e_1=(1,0)\) and \(\vec e_2=(0,1)\text{.}\) Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F(\vec e_1) = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\\3\end{bmatrix}, \qquad F(\vec e_2) = \begin{bmatrix}4\\5\\6\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Hence the matrix of \(F\) (with respect to the standard bases of \(\mathbb{R}^2\) and \(\mathbb{R}^3\)) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} [F] \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 4\\ 2 \amp 5\\ 3 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

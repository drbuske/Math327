    Handout Daily Prep 2.4 - Mechanical Vibrations
    

    Section Overview

    In this section, we explore how second‑order linear constant‑coefficient differential equations model real‑world physical systems, especially mechanical vibrations. The text introduces the mass‑spring‑damper system, explains how forces such as stiffness, damping, and external inputs shape the motion, and shows how Newton’s laws lead naturally to differential equations of the form \(m x'' + c x' + kx = F(t)\text{.}\) We then see how similar mathematical structures appear in electrical RLC circuits and pendulum motion, highlighting the unifying theme that diverse physical phenomena can be modeled and analyzed using the same ODE framework.
    🔗
    🔗

    These are the tasks you should be able to perform with reasonable fluency when you arrive at our next class meeting. Important new vocabulary words are indicated in italics.
    🔗
    • Identify how mass–spring–damper systems give rise to second‑order linear constant‑coefficient ODEs.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Distinguish between forced/unforced and damped/undamped motions in mechanical systems.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Recognize that electrical RLC circuits follow the same mathematical form as mechanical vibration models.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Understanding the Basic Model.
    The differential equation for a mass–spring–damper system is \(m x'' + c x' + k x = F(t)\text{.}\) What does the constant \(c\) represent?
    🔗
    • The damping coefficient.
    • Correct. \(c \ge 0\) is the friction/damping term.
    • The spring stiffness.
    • No—that is \(k\text{.}\)
    • The external forcing strength.
    • No—forcing is represented by \(F(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    2. Forced vs. Unforced Motion.
    In the model \(m x'' + c x' + k x = F(t)\text{,}\) when is the motion called unforced?
    🔗
    • When \(F(t) \equiv 0\text{.}\)
    • Correct. If \(F(t)\) is identically zero, the motion is unforced (free).
    • When \(c=0\text{.}\)
    • No—that corresponds to undamped, not unforced.
    • When \(k=0\text{.}\)
    • No—that would eliminate the spring.
    🔗
    3. Damped vs. Undamped.
    In the ODE \(m x'' + c x' + k x = 0\text{,}\) how do we know whether the system is damped?
    🔗
    • If \(c > 0\text{,}\) the motion is damped.
    • Correct. \(c > 0\) means damping is present.
    • If \(m=0\text{.}\)
    • No—a mass must be positive in the model.
    • If \(k=0\text{.}\)
    • No—the spring constant has nothing to do with damping.
    🔗
    4. Qualitative Behavior of Undamped Motion.
    Consider the unforced, undamped equation \(m x'' + k x = 0\text{.}\) What type of motion does it describe?
    🔗
    • Simple harmonic oscillations with constant amplitude.
    • Correct. With \(c=0\) and \(F(t)=0\text{,}\) the solution is sinusoidal. This is undamped harmonic motion.
    • Damped oscillations that decay over time.
    • No—that requires \(c>0\text{.}\)
    • Exponential growth without oscillations.
    • No—the restoring force \(k x\) prevents runaway motion.
    🔗
    5. Natural Frequency.
    For the undamped, unforced system \(m x'' + k x = 0\text{,}\) the angular frequency of oscillation is \(\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\text{.}\) If \(m = 4\) and \(k = 36\text{,}\) what is \(\omega\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(\omega = 3\)
    • Correct. \(\omega = \sqrt{36/4} = \sqrt{9} = 3.\)
    • \(\omega = 6\)
    • This corresponds to forgetting the division by \(m\text{.}\)
    • \(\omega = \sqrt{40}\)
    • Incorrect substitution into the formula.
    🔗
    6.
    Consider the spring–mass system shown in Figure 17. An object of mass \(m\) is attached to a spring with spring constant \(k\text{,}\) and a dashpot provides damping with coefficient \(c\text{.}\) If \(x(t)\) denotes displacement from equilibrium, then
    🔗
    \begin{equation} m \frac{d^{2}x}{dt^{2}} + c \frac{dx}{dt} + kx = 0.\tag{0} \end{equation}
    A dashpot exerts a resistive force proportional to the velocity. We investigate several cases of the equation depending on \(m\text{,}\) \(c\text{,}\) and \(k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Undamped Motion. Suppose \(c=0\text{.}\) Then the model becomes
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} m x'' + kx = 0. \end{equation*}
      With \(\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}\text{:}\)
      🔗
      1. Find the characteristic equation and its roots.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Find the general solution \(x(t)\) written in terms of \(\omega_0\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    2. Undamped Motion (Example). A mass \(m=\tfrac12\) kg is attached to a spring that is stretched 2 m by a force of 100 N. Initial conditions: \(x(0)=1\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=-5\text{.}\)
      🔗
      1. Find the spring constant \(k\) and write the resulting DE.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Interpret the differential equation in words.
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Explain what the initial conditions mean physically.
        🔗
        🔗
      4. Given \(\omega_0 = 10\text{,}\) the solution is \(x(t) = \cos(10t) - \tfrac12 \sin(10t)\text{.}\) Compute the frequency \(\nu = \omega_0/(2\pi)\) and the period \(T\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      5. Convert \(x(t) = c_1 \cos(\omega_0 t) + c_2 \sin(\omega_0 t)\) into \(x(t) = A \cos(\omega_0 t - \phi)\text{.}\)
        🔗
        1. Compute \(A = \sqrt{c_1^2 + c_2^2}\text{.}\)
          🔗
          🔗
        2. Compute \(\phi\) from \(\tan\phi = c_2/c_1\) (noting it lies in quadrant IV).
          🔗
          🔗
        🔗
      6. The first peak occurs at \(t^* = \phi/\omega_0\text{.}\) Determine \(t^*\) and describe/sketch the motion.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    3. With damping (\(c > 0\)), the characteristic equation is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} m r^2 + c r + k = 0, \end{equation*}
      with roots
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} r = \frac{-c \pm \sqrt{c^2 - 4mk}}{2m}. \end{equation*}
      • Overdamped: \(c^2 > 4mk\)
        🔗
      • Critically damped: \(c^2 = 4mk\)
        🔗
      • Underdamped: \(c^2 < 4mk\)
        🔗
      🔗
    4. Overdamped Motion. Assume \(c^2 > 4mk\text{.}\)
      🔗
      1. Explain why both roots \(r_1,r_2\) are negative.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Give the general solution.
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Compute \(\lim_{t\to\infty} x(t)\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    5. Critically Damped Motion. Assume \(c^2 = 4mk\text{.}\)
      🔗
      1. Explain why the repeated root is \(r=-c/(2m)\) and why it is negative.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Give the general solution.
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Compute \(\lim_{t\to\infty} x(t)\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    6. Underdamped Motion. Suppose \(c=1\text{,}\) \(m=\tfrac12\text{,}\) \(k=50\text{.}\)
      🔗
      1. Find the complex roots of the characteristic equation.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Given the solution
        🔗
        \begin{equation*} x(t) = e^{-t}\!\Bigl( \cos(\sqrt{99}t) - \frac{4}{\sqrt{99}}\sin(\sqrt{99}t) \Bigr), \end{equation*}
        Compute \(\lim_{t\to\infty} x(t)\) and describe the qualitative shape of the graph.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    (a) Undamped Motion (c = 0)
    🔗
    1. The DE is \(m x'' + kx = 0\text{.}\) Characteristic equation:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} mr^{2} + k = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad r^{2} = -\frac{k}{m}. \end{equation*}
      Thus \(r = \pm i\sqrt{k/m} = \pm i\omega_0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The general solution is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} x(t) = C_1 \cos(\omega_0 t) + C_2 \sin(\omega_0 t). \end{equation*}
      🔗
    (b) Undamped Example
    🔗
    1. Hooke’s law: a force of 100 N stretches the spring 2 m, so
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} k = \frac{100}{2} = 50. \end{equation*}
      The DE is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \tfrac12 x'' + 50 x = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x'' + 100 x = 0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. The DE states: acceleration is proportional to displacement toward equilibrium.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The initial conditions mean the mass starts 1 m to the right of equilibrium and moving left at 5 m/s.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Frequency:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \nu = \omega_{0}/(2\pi) = 10/(2\pi) = \frac{5}{\pi} \approx 1.59\ \text{Hz}. \end{equation*}
      Period:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} T = 1/\nu = \frac{\pi}{5} \approx 0.628. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    5. Here \(c_1 = 1\text{,}\) \(c_2 = -\tfrac12\text{.}\)
      🔗
      1. \begin{align*} A \amp = \sqrt{1^2 + \left(-\tfrac12\right)^2} \\ \amp = \sqrt{1 + \tfrac14} \\ \amp = \sqrt{\tfrac54} \\ \amp = \tfrac{\sqrt5}{2}. \end{align*}
        🔗
      2. \(\tan\phi = c_2/c_1 = -\tfrac12\text{.}\) Quadrant IV → \(\phi \approx -0.46365\) radians or equivalently \(\phi = 2\pi - 0.46365 \approx 5.8195\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    6. Time lag:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} t^{*} = \phi/\omega_{0} \approx 5.8195 / 10 = 0.58195. \end{equation*}
      The graph is a cosine‑like oscillation of amplitude \(A\) and frequency \(\nu\text{,}\) shifted right.
      🔗
      🔗
    (c) Overdamped Motion
    🔗
    1. For \(c^{2} > 4mk\text{,}\) \(r = \frac{-c \pm \sqrt{c^{2}-4mk}}{2m}\text{.}\) Since \(c > \sqrt{c^{2}-4mk} > 0\text{,}\) both numerator terms are negative ⇒ both roots negative.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. General solution:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} x(t) = C_1 e^{r_1 t} + C_2 e^{r_2 t} \quad (r_1,r_2 < 0). \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Limit:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t) = 0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    (d) Critically Damped Motion
    🔗
    1. If \(c^2 = 4mk\text{,}\) \(r = \frac{-c}{2m}\text{.}\) Since \(c > 0,m > 0\text{,}\) the root is negative.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. General solution:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)e^{rt}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Limit:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t) = 0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    (e) Underdamped Motion
    🔗
    1. Characteristic equation:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \tfrac12 r^{2} + 1 r + 50 = 0. \end{equation*}
      Multiply by 2: \(r^{2} + 2r + 100 = 0\text{.}\) Roots:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} r = -1 \pm i\sqrt{99}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. For the solution
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} x(t) = e^{-t}\!\left( \cos\sqrt{99}t - \frac{4}{\sqrt{99}}\sin\sqrt{99}t \right), \end{equation*}
      the exponential factor dominates:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t) = 0. \end{equation*}
      Qualitatively, the plot shows oscillations whose amplitude decays exponentially.
      🔗
      🔗

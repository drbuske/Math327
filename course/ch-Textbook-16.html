    Handout Daily Prep 3.3 - Convolution
    

    Section Overview

    In this section we learn about the convolution of two functions, a construction that lets us combine functions in a way that behaves very much like a product while being compatible with the Laplace transform. The section introduces the definition of convolution as an integral from 0 to \(t\text{,}\) works through examples such as convolving exponentials or trigonometric functions, and highlights key algebraic properties including commutativity and associativity. Most importantly, we will see that the Laplace transform of a convolution is the product of the individual Laplace transforms, a fact that allows us to compute inverse Laplace transforms and solve differential equations involving general forcing functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Definition of Convolution.
    The convolution of two functions \(f(t)\) and \(g(t)\) (for \(t \ge 0\)) is defined as:
    🔗
    • \((f * g)(t) = \int_{0}^{t} f(\tau)\, g(t-\tau)\, d\tau\)
    • Correct. This is exactly the definition of convolution.
    • \((f * g)(t) = f(t)g(t)\)
    • No—convolution is not pointwise multiplication.
    • \((f * g)(t) = \int_{0}^{\infty} f(\tau)g(\tau)\, d\tau\)
    • No—this describes an inner product, not convolution.
    🔗
    2. Understanding Example 3.3.1.
    In Example 3.3.1, the convolution of \(f(t)=e^{t}\) and \(g(t)=t\) is computed. Which statement below is true about \((f * g)(t)\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • It equals \(e^{t}(t-1)\) after evaluating the integral.
    • Correct. Example 3.3.1 shows that \((f*g)(t)=\int_{0}^{t} e^{\tau}(t-\tau)\, d\tau = e^{t}(t-1)\text{.}\)
    • It equals \(t\,e^{t}\) with no subtraction.
    • No—an integration by parts yields a \(-e^{t}\) correction term.
    • It equals \(t^{2}\text{.}\)
    • No—there is no polynomial result here.
    🔗
    3. Laplace Transform of a Convolution.
    What does the Convolution Theorem state?
    🔗
    • \({\cal L}\{f * g\}(s) = F(s)\,G(s)\)
    • Correct. The Laplace transform of a convolution is the product of the transforms.
    • \({\cal L}\{f * g\}(s) = F(s) + G(s)\)
    • No—the theorem involves multiplication, not addition.
    • \({\cal L}\{f * g\}(s) = \dfrac{F(s)}{G(s)}\)
    • No—division does not appear in the convolution theorem.
    🔗
    4. Basic Properties of Convolution.
    Which of the following is a true property of convolution?
    🔗
    • \(f * g = g * f\)
    • Correct. Convolution is commutative.
    • \((f * g)(t) = f(t)\text{.}\)
    • No—this is false unless \(g\) is a delta function.
    • \(f * (g+h) = (f*g) - (f*h)\)
    • No—convolution distributes over addition with a plus, not a minus.
    🔗
    5. Using the Convolution Theorem.
    To compute \({\cal L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{1}{(s+1)s^{2}}\right\}\text{,}\) we can recognize the expression as
    🔗
    • The product \(\dfrac{1}{s+1}\cdot \dfrac{1}{s^{2}}\text{,}\) whose inverse transform is the convolution of \(e^{-t}\) and \(t\text{.}\)
    • Correct. \({\cal L}^{-1}\{1/(s+1)s^{2}\} = (e^{-t} * t)(t)\text{.}\)
    • The sum of two transforms.
    • No—it is a product of transforms.
    • A quotient \(\dfrac{1/s}{1/(s+1)}\text{.}\)
    • No—we do not use division for convolution.
    🔗
    6.
    Theorem 3.3.1 sheds light on why we care to bother defining the convolution. In your own words, describe the purpose of defining a convolution product.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The convolution is defined so that its Laplace transform becomes the product of the Laplace transforms of the original functions. In other words,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\cal L}\{(f * g)(t)\} = F(s)\,G(s), \end{equation*}
    which is Theorem 3.3.1. This is extremely useful because it lets us take an inverse Laplace transform of a product by converting that product into a convolution integral of simpler functions whose inverse transforms we already know. The convolution therefore provides a way to solve differential equations and compute inverse transforms that would be very difficult otherwise.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    Let \(f(t)=t^{2}\) for \(t\ge 0\) and \(g(t)=u(t-1)\text{.}\) Compute the convolution product \((f * g)(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    By definition of convolution,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f * g)(t) = \int_{0}^{t} f(\tau)\, g(t-\tau)\, d\tau = \int_{0}^{t} \tau^{2}\, u(t-\tau-1)\, d\tau. \end{equation*}
    The factor \(u(t-\tau-1)\) equals 1 exactly when \(t - \tau \ge 1\text{,}\) i.e. \(\tau \le t-1\text{.}\) Therefore the integral becomes:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f * g)(t) = \int_{0}^{t-1} \tau^{2}\, d\tau \quad \text{for } t \ge 1,\qquad (f * g)(t) = 0 \text{ for } t < 1. \end{equation*}
    Compute the integral:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{t-1} \tau^{2}\, d\tau = \left. \frac{\tau^{3}}{3} \right|_{0}^{\,t-1} = \frac{(t-1)^{3}}{3}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f * g)(t) = \begin{cases} 0, \amp t < 1,\\[6pt] \dfrac{(t-1)^{3}}{3}, \amp t \ge 1. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    Solve the Volterra integral equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = e^{-t} + \int_{0}^{t} \cos(t-\tau)\, x(\tau)\, d\tau. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    1. Apply the Laplace transform.
    🔗
    Let \(X(s)={\cal L}\{x(t)\}\text{.}\) Using the convolution theorem:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\cal L}\!\left[\int_{0}^{t} \cos(t-\tau)x(\tau)\,d\tau\right] = {\cal L}\{\cos t\}\, X(s) = \frac{s}{s^{2}+1} X(s). \end{equation*}
    Also \({\cal L}\{e^{-t}\} = \dfrac{1}{s+1}\text{.}\) Taking the Laplace transform of the entire equation gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{s+1} + \frac{s}{s^{2}+1} X(s). \end{equation*}
    2. Solve for \(X(s)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s)\!\left(1 - \frac{s}{s^{2}+1}\right) = \frac{1}{s+1}. \end{equation*}
    Compute the factor:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1 - \frac{s}{s^{2}+1} = \frac{s^{2}+1 - s}{s^{2}+1} = \frac{s^{2}-s+1}{s^{2}+1}. \end{equation*}
    Thus:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{s+1}\,\frac{s^{2}+1}{\,s^{2}-s+1\,}. \end{equation*}
    3. Invert the transform.
    🔗
    Rewrite \(s^{2}+1\) to match the denominator:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s^{2}+1 = (s^{2}-s+1) + s. \end{equation*}
    Therefore:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{s+1} \left( 1 + \frac{s}{s^{2}-s+1} \right) = \frac{1}{s+1} + \frac{s}{(s+1)(s^{2}-s+1)}. \end{equation*}
    The inverse Laplace transform of \(1/(s+1)\) is \(e^{-t}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    4. Invert the second term.
    🔗
    Note that \(s^{2}-s+1 = (s-\tfrac12)^{2} + \tfrac34\text{.}\) Thus the denominator corresponds to a damped sinusoid:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\cal L}^{-1}\!\left[\frac{s-\frac12}{(s-\frac12)^{2} + (\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})^{2}}\right] = e^{t/2}\cos\!\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right), \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} {\cal L}^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{(s-\frac12)^{2} + (\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})^{2}}\right] = e^{t/2}\sin\!\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right). \end{equation*}
    Carefully decomposing the fraction and inverting gives the final solution:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = e^{-t} \;+\; \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\,e^{-t/2}\, \sin\!\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right). \end{equation*}
    One may check directly (by differentiating and substituting) that this function satisfies the original integral equation.

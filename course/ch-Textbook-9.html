    Handout Daily Prep 2.1 - Second Order Linear ODEs
    

    Section Overview

    In this section, we will learn how to recognize, interpret, and solve second‑order linear ordinary differential equations, which take the general form \(A(x) y'' + B(x) y' + C(x) y = F(x)\text{.}\) After rewriting such equations in the standardized form \(y'' + p(x) y' + q(x) y = f(x)\text{,}\) we will explore the foundational structure of linear ODEs, including the distinction between homogeneous and nonhomogeneous equations and the crucial fact that no powers or nonlinear functions of \(y\text{,}\) \(y'\text{,}\) or \(y''\) appear. A major focus is understanding the principle of superposition, which allows solutions of homogeneous equations to be combined into new solutions, and learning how this principle, along with existence and uniqueness results, gives linear ODEs a powerful and predictable solution theory. We will also be introduced to the operator viewpoint \(L y = y'' + p y' + q y\text{,}\) which provides an elegant framework for understanding why linear equations behave so systematically.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Definition of a Second-Order Linear ODE.
    Which of the following differential equations is linear and second‑order?
    🔗
    • \(y'' + p(x)\,y' + q(x)\,y = f(x)\)
    • Correct. A general second‑order linear ODE is defined as \(A(x)y'' + B(x)y' + C(x)y = F(x)\text{.}\)
    • \(y'' = (y')^{2} + x\)
    • Not linear: it contains \((y')^{2}\text{.}\)
    • \(y' + p(x)\,y = f(x)\)
    • This is first‑order, not second‑order.
    🔗
    2. Homogeneous vs. Nonhomogeneous.
    Which of the following is a homogeneous second‑order linear equation?
    🔗
    • \(y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0\)
    • Correct. A homogeneous equation has \(f(x)=0\) on the right-hand side.
    • \(y'' + 3y' + y = \sin x\)
    • Not homogeneous: \(\sin x\) is a forcing term.
    • \(y'' = y\cos x + 1\)
    • Also nonhomogeneous due to the “+1”.
    🔗
    3. Recognizing Solutions of a Homogeneous Equation.
    Suppose \(y'' + k^{2}y = 0\text{.}\) Which pair of functions forms a fundamental set of solutions?
    🔗
    • \(\cos(kx)\) and \(\sin(kx)\)
    • Correct. \(y_1=\cos(kx)\) and \(y_2=\sin(kx)\) are two solutions of this equation.
    • \(e^{kx}\) and \(1\)
    • This pair does not satisfy \(y''+k^{2}y=0\text{.}\)
    • \(e^{kx}\) and \(e^{-kx}\)
    • These solve \(y'' - k^{2}y = 0\text{,}\) not \(y'' + k^{2}y = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    4. The Superposition Principle.
    If \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are solutions to the homogeneous equation \(y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0\text{,}\) what else must be a solution?
    🔗
    • \(C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2\) for any constants \(C_1, C_2\)
    • Correct. The superposition principle states that any linear combination of solutions is also a solution.
    • \(y_1 y_2\)
    • Products of solutions do not generally solve the equation.
    • \(y_1 - y_2^2\)
    • Nonlinear combinations break linearity of the operator.
    🔗
    5. Checking a Proposed Solution.
    Check whether \(y(x)=e^{2x}\) is a solution of \(y'' - 4y = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • Yes, because \(y'' - 4y = (4e^{2x}) - 4e^{2x} = 0\text{.}\)
    • Correct. Differentiating twice gives \(y''=4e^{2x}\text{,}\) and the equation is satisfied.
    • No, because \(y'' - 4y = 8e^{2x}\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect computation of the derivatives.
    • No, exponentials cannot solve homogeneous equations.
    • False—exponentials are key solutions in Section 2.1.
    🔗
    6.
    Define \(L\) by \(L(y) = y'' - y'\text{.}\) Let \(y_{1}(x) = e^{x}\) and \(y_{2}(x) = e^{2x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Compute \(L(y_{1}(x))\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Compute \(3 \cdot L(y_{2}(x))\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Compute \(L\!\bigl(y_{1}(x) + 3y_{2}(x)\bigr)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. How does your answer to (c) compare to the sum of your answers to (a) and (b)? Is \(L\) linear?
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. Compute \(L(y_1)\text{.}\) Since \(y_1' = e^{x}\) and \(y_1'' = e^{x}\text{,}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} L(y_1) = y_1'' - y_1' = e^{x} - e^{x} = 0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. For \(y_2(x)=e^{2x}\text{,}\) we have \(y_2' = 2e^{2x}\) and \(y_2'' = 4e^{2x}\text{.}\) So
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} L(y_2) = y_2'' - y_2' = 4e^{2x} - 2e^{2x} = 2e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
      Therefore,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} 3L(y_2) = 6e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Compute \(L(y_1 + 3y_2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} (y_1 + 3y_2)' = e^{x} + 6e^{2x}, \qquad (y_1 + 3y_2)'' = e^{x} + 12e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
      \begin{align*} L(y_1 + 3y_2) \amp = (e^{x} + 12e^{2x}) - (e^{x} + 6e^{2x}) \\ \amp = 6e^{2x}. \end{align*}
      🔗
    4. The answer to (c) equals the sum of (a) and (b):
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} 0 + 6e^{2x} = 6e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
      Thus \(L\) satisfies \(L(y_1 + 3y_2) = L(y_1) + 3L(y_2)\text{,}\) showing that \(L\) is linear.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    Consider the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0. \end{equation*}
    Define the operator \(L\) by \(L(y) = y'' - 3y' + 2y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Show that \(y_{1}(x)=e^{x}\) and \(y_{2}(x)=e^{2x}\) solve the equation by computing \(L(y_{1})\) and \(L(y_{2})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. For constants \(c_1,c_2\text{,}\) compute \(L(c_1y_1 + c_2y_2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Does \(y(x)=c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{2x}\) solve the differential equation?
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. For \(y_1=e^{x}\text{,}\) \(y_1'=e^{x},\;y_1''=e^{x}\text{.}\) Thus:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} L(y_1) = e^{x} - 3e^{x} + 2e^{x} = 0. \end{equation*}
      For \(y_2=e^{2x}\text{,}\) \(y_2'=2e^{2x},\;y_2''=4e^{2x}\text{.}\) So:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} L(y_2) = 4e^{2x} - 6e^{2x} + 2e^{2x} = 0. \end{equation*}
      Both functions satisfy the differential equation.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use linearity of derivatives:
      🔗
      \begin{align*} L(c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2) \amp = c_1 L(y_1) + c_2 L(y_2) \\ \amp = c_1 \cdot 0 + c_2 \cdot 0 \\ \amp = 0. \end{align*}
      🔗
    3. Yes. Because \(L(c_1y_1 + c_2y_2)=0\) for any constants \(c_1,c_2\text{,}\) the general solution of this linear second‑order homogeneous equation is \(y(x) = c_1 e^{x} + c_2 e^{2x}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    Consider the differential equation \(2y'' - 7y' + 3y = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Are \(y_1(x)=e^{x/2}\) and \(y_2(x)=e^{3x}\) linearly dependent or independent? Explain.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Describe the general solution \(y(x)\) to this differential equation.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. The functions \(e^{x/2}\) and \(e^{3x}\) cannot be constant multiples of each other, since \(\frac{x}{2} \ne 3x\) for all \(x\text{.}\) Therefore they are linearly independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. For a second‑order homogeneous linear ODE, any two linearly independent solutions form a basis. Hence the general solution is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{x/2} + C_2 e^{3x}. \end{equation*}
      This describes every solution to \(2y'' - 7y' + 3y = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗

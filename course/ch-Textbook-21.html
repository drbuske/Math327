    Section Overview

    This section introduces the idea that certain collections of vectors arising in linear algebraâ€”particularly the set of solutions to a homogeneous linear equationâ€”form what are known as subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\text{.}\) A subspace is any subset closed under vector addition and scalar multiplication, so every linear combination of its vectors remains inside it. The notes show that familiar examples such as \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\text{,}\) the zero vector alone, or sets like \(\{(a,a): a \in \mathbb{R}\}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\) all satisfy these properties. A subspace can always be described as the span of some set of vectors, and if that spanning set is linearly independent, it forms a basis. The number of basis vectors defines the subspaceâ€™s dimension, a quantity that is unique even though many different bases may exist. This viewpoint emphasizes that subspaces behave like smaller Euclidean spaces embedded inside higher-dimensional ones and that vectors in a \(k\)â€‘dimensional subspace can be coordinatized using \(k\) numbers once a basis is chosen.

    3. Dimension Reasoning with a Kernel.
    Let \(L:\mathbb{R}^{4} \to \mathbb{R}^{3}\) be a linear transformation. Suppose the matrix of \(L\) (in standard coordinates) has rank \(2\text{.}\) Which of the following statements must be true?
    ðŸ”—

    • Incorrect. \(\ker(L)\) is a \(2\)â€‘dimensional subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^{4}\text{,}\) not literally \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\text{.}\) Subspaces â€œlook likeâ€ \(\mathbb{R}^{k}\) only in abstract structure, not as literal coordinate spaces.

    7.

    8.

    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Solving the system:
    ðŸ”—
    From \(x + y = 0\) we get \(x = -y\text{.}\) From \(y + z = 0\) we get \(z = -y\text{.}\) Letting \(y = t\text{,}\) a free variable, we obtain \((x,y,z) = (-t,\, t,\, -t) = t(-1,1,-1).\)

    9.
    Consider an arbitrary subset \(S \subset \mathbb{R}^{n}\text{.}\) Students often think that a subspace must â€œlook geometricâ€ (a line through the origin, a plane through the origin, etc.). Provide a conceptual explanationâ€”without checking closure conditions on any specific examplesâ€”of why requiring closure under all linear combinations (not just addition or scalar multiplication alone) is the essential feature that distinguishes subspaces from general subsets.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    A subspace must behave like a smaller copy of \(\mathbb{R}^{k}\) living inside \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\text{,}\) meaning that vectors can be combined in exactly the same ways we can combine ordinary vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\)â€”by forming any linear combination.
    ðŸ”—
    If a subset is closed under both addition and scalar multiplication, then it is automatically closed under all linear combinations (because \(a\vec{x}+ b\vec{y}\) is simply repeated use of these two operations). The section emphasizes that this â€œlinearâ€“combination stabilityâ€ is what makes the solution set of a homogeneous linear system behave like a Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^{k}\) and justifies calling it a subspace. Without this full closure property, the set would not support the algebraic structure required for bases, dimension, or unique coordinate representations.

    10.
    The notes state that while the dimension of a subspace is unique, the basis is not. Explain conceptuallyâ€”without doing any computationsâ€”why a subspace can have many different bases, but cannot have more than one dimension. Use ideas from the section to justify why different bases still span â€œthe same amount of space.â€
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Every basis of a subspace \(S\) has two defining features: the vectors must be linearly independent, and they must span all of \(S\)
    ðŸ”—
    Many different choices of spanning vectors can achieve this job, because starting from one basis we can replace vectors with new ones obtained from linear combinations, as long as we maintain independence and still span the whole set. Therefore, many bases exist.
    ðŸ”—
    However, the number of vectors in any basis is fixed: it is the dimension of the subspace. If one basis had more vectors than another, that would contradict the meaning of â€œlinearly independent spanning set,â€ since the section explains that a subspace \(S\) has a unique positive integer \(k\) such that every vector in \(S\) can be written uniquely as a combination of \(k\) independent basis vectors. This uniqueness of representation forces the dimension to be unique, even though the choice of basis is not.

    11.
    The kernel of a linear transformation \(L : \mathbb{R}^{n} \to \mathbb{R}^{m}\) is defined as the set \(\ker(L) = \{\vec{x}: L\vec{x}= \vec{0}\}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Provide a conceptual explanation of why the kernel must always be a subspace, and describe what this tells us about the â€œshapeâ€ of solution sets to homogeneous linear equations.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Because \(L\) is linear, \(L(\vec{x}+ \vec{y}) = L\vec{x}+ L\vec{y}\) and \(L(\alpha \vec{x}) = \alpha L\vec{x}\) for all scalars \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If \(\vec{x}\) and \(\vec{y}\) are in the kernel, then \(L\vec{x}= \vec{0}\) and \(L\vec{y}= \vec{0}\text{,}\) so \(L(\vec{x}+ \vec{y}) = \vec{0}\) and \(L(\alpha \vec{x}) = \vec{0}\text{.}\) Thus the kernel contains every linear combination of its vectors; it is automatically a subspace.
    ðŸ”—
    Conceptually, this means the solution set to any homogeneous linear system â€œinheritsâ€ the geometry of a Euclidean subspace: it behaves like a flat space of some dimension \(k\text{,}\) possibly much smaller than \(n\text{.}\) The notes highlight this by explaining that kernels look and act like \(\mathbb{R}^{k}\) once a basis has been chosen, and vectors in the kernel have unique coordinate representations in terms of that basis.

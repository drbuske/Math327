    Exercises Questions

    1.
    Consider the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0. \end{equation*}
    1. Explain why this differential equation is a second-order linear homogeneous differential equation.
      🔗
    2. Verify directly that \(y_1(x) = e^{x}\) and \(y_2(x) = e^{2x}\) are solutions.
      🔗
    3. Using (b), explain how you know that \(y(x) = 5e^{x} - 3e^{2x}\) is also a solution.
      🔗
    🔗
    Hint.
    For part (a), recall the definitions.
    🔗
    For parts (b) and (c), compute \(y'\) and \(y''\) and substitute into the ODE to check whether the left-hand side equals zero.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The equation is second‑order linear homogeneous.
    • “Second order” means \(y''\) appears.
      🔗
    • “Linear” means \(y\text{,}\) \(y'\text{,}\) \(y''\) appear only to the first power and are not multiplied together.
      🔗
    • “Homogeneous” means the right-hand side is zero.
      🔗
    Substitution shows that both \(e^{x}\) and \(e^{2x}\) satisfy the ODE. By the superposition principle, any linear combination such as \(5e^{x} - 3e^{2x}\) is also a solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) The equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0 \end{equation*}
    is second order because it involves \(y''\text{.}\) It is linear because \(y\text{,}\) \(y'\text{,}\) and \(y''\) appear only to the first power and are not multiplied by one another or placed inside nonlinear functions. It is homogeneous because the right-hand side is zero.
    🔗
    (b) Verify that \(y_1(x)=e^{x}\) is a solution. Compute derivatives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_1' = e^{x}, \qquad y_1'' = e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute into the left-hand side of the ODE:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_1'' - 3y_1' + 2y_1 = e^{x} - 3e^{x} + 2e^{x} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(e^{x}\) is a solution.
    🔗
    Now verify \(y_2(x)=e^{2x}\text{.}\) Compute derivatives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_2' = 2e^{2x}, \qquad y_2'' = 4e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_2'' - 3y_2' + 2y_2 = 4e^{2x} - 6e^{2x} + 2e^{2x} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(e^{2x}\) is also a solution.
    🔗
    (c) Because the equation is linear and homogeneous, any linear combination of solutions is again a solution. Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = 5e^{x} - 3e^{2x} \end{equation*}
    must satisfy the ODE. For completeness, substitute:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y'' - 3y' + 2y \amp = 5( e^{x} - 3e^{x} + 2e^{x} ) \;-\; 3( 4e^{2x} - 6e^{2x} + 2e^{2x} ) \\ \amp = 5 \cdot 0 - 3 \cdot 0 \\ \amp = 0. \end{align*}
    Hence \(y(x)=5e^{x} - 3e^{2x}\) is indeed a solution of the differential equation.
    🔗
    🔗

    2.
    Consider the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - y' - 6y = 0. \end{equation*}
    1. Form the characteristic equation and find its roots.
      🔗
    2. Write down the general solution of the differential equation.
      🔗
    3. Find the particular solution satisfying the initial conditions \(y(0)=3\) and \(y'(0)=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    Hint.
    The associated characteristic equation is \(r^2 - r - 6 = 0.\)
    🔗
    Factor the quadratic to find two distinct real roots. When the characteristic roots are \(r_1\) and \(r_2\text{,}\) the general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{r_1 x} + C_2 e^{r_2 x}. \end{equation*}
    To apply initial conditions, compute \(y'(x)\) and substitute \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The characteristic equation is \(r^2 - r - 6 = 0\text{,}\) which factors to \((r-3)(r+2)=0\text{,}\) giving roots \(r_1=3\) and \(r_2=-2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{3x} + C_2 e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The initial conditions give \(C_1 = \tfrac{6}{5}\) and \(C_2 = \tfrac{9}{5}\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = \frac{6}{5}e^{3x} + \frac{9}{5}e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) We form the characteristic equation by replacing \(y''\) with \(r^2\text{,}\) \(y'\) with \(r\text{,}\) and \(y\) with \(1\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r^2 - r - 6 = 0. \end{equation*}
    This quadratic factors as \((r - 3)(r + 2) = 0,\) giving the real distinct roots \(r_1 = 3\) and \(r_2 = -2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) When the characteristic equation has real distinct roots, the general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{3x} + C_2 e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    (c) Apply the initial condition \(y(0)=3\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(0) = C_1 + C_2 = 3. \end{equation*}
    Compute \(y'(x)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(x) = 3C_1 e^{3x} - 2C_2 e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    Apply \(y'(0)=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 3C_1 - 2C_2 = 0. \end{equation*}
    We now solve the system:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C_1 + C_2 = 3, \qquad 3C_1 - 2C_2 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solving:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C_1 = \frac{6}{5}, \quad C_2 = \frac{9}{5}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the particular solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = \frac{6}{5}e^{3x} + \frac{9}{5}e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    Consider the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 4y' + 5y = 0. \end{equation*}
    1. Find the general solution.
      🔗
    2. Determine the particular solution satisfying the initial conditions \(y(0) = 2\) and \(y'(0) = 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    Hint.
    Form the characteristic equation associated with \(y'' - 4y' + 5y = 0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r^{2} - 4r + 5 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solve this quadratic to obtain complex roots of the form \(r = \alpha \pm \beta i\text{.}\) When the roots are complex, the general solution has the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = e^{\alpha x}\big(C_{1}\cos(\beta x) + C_{2}\sin(\beta x)\big). \end{equation*}
    Then apply the initial conditions to solve for \(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = e^{2x}\big(C_{1}\cos x + C_{2}\sin x\big). \end{equation*}
    The initial conditions give \(C_{1}=2\) and \(C_{2}=-3\text{,}\) so the particular solution is
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = e^{2x}\big(2\cos x - 3\sin x\big). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) To solve the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 4y' + 5y = 0, \end{equation*}
    we form the characteristic equation \(r^{2} - 4r + 5 = 0.\)
    🔗
    Solving this quadratic:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} r \amp = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{(-4)^{2} - 4(1)(5)}}{2} \\ \amp = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 20}}{2} \\ \amp = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2} \\ \amp = \frac{4 \pm 2i}{2} \\ \amp = 2 \pm i. \end{align*}
    Since the roots are complex with real part \(2\) and imaginary part \(1\text{,}\) the general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = e^{2x}\big(C_{1}\cos x + C_{2}\sin x\big). \end{equation*}
    (b) Apply the initial conditions. First evaluate \(y(0)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(0) = e^{0}\big(C_{1}\cos 0 + C_{2}\sin 0\big) = C_{1}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(y(0)=2\text{,}\) we obtain \(C_{1}=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next compute \(y'(x)\text{.}\) Differentiating,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(x) = e^{2x}\big[2(C_{1}\cos x + C_{2}\sin x) - C_{1}\sin x + C_{2}\cos x\big]. \end{equation*}
    Evaluate at \(x=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y'(0) \amp = e^{0}\big[2(C_{1}\cos 0 + C_{2}\sin 0) - C_{1}\sin 0 + C_{2}\cos 0\big] \\ \amp = 2C_{1} + C_{2}. \end{align*}
    Using \(y'(0)=1\) and \(C_{1}=2\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1 = 2(2) + C_{2} = 4 + C_{2}, \end{equation*}
    so \(C_{2} = -3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore the solution satisfying the initial conditions is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = e^{2x}\big(2\cos x - 3\sin x\big). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    A fourth-order linear homogeneous differential equation has the characteristic equation with roots
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r = 4,\quad r = 1,\quad r = 1,\quad r = -2. \end{equation*}
    Write down the general solution of the differential equation.
    🔗
    Hint.
    If \(r\) is a simple root of the characteristic equation, then \(e^{rx}\) is a solution.
    🔗
    If \(r\) is a repeated root of multiplicity \(m\text{,}\) then the corresponding solutions are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^{rx},\quad x e^{rx},\quad x^{2} e^{rx},\;\ldots,\; x^{m-1} e^{rx}. \end{equation*}
    Combine all corresponding terms with arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_{1} e^{4x} + C_{2} e^{x} + C_{3} x e^{x} + C_{4} e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The given roots of the characteristic equation are:
    🔗
    • \(r=4\) (simple root),
      🔗
    • \(r=1\) (repeated twice),
      🔗
    • \(r=-2\) (simple root).
      🔗
    For the simple roots \(r=4\) and \(r=-2\text{,}\) the solutions are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^{4x},\qquad e^{-2x}. \end{equation*}
    For the repeated root \(r=1\) of multiplicity 2, we take
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^{x},\qquad x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Combining all four linearly independent solutions, the general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_{1} e^{4x} + C_{2} e^{x} + C_{3} x e^{x} + C_{4} e^{-2x}, \end{equation*}
    where \(C_1, C_2, C_3, C_4\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    A fifth-order linear nonhomogeneous differential equation has homogeneous part associated with the factored characteristic equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (r - 2)\,\bigl( (r + 1 - 3i)(r + 1 + 3i) \bigr)^{2} = 0. \end{equation*}
    You are told that a particular solution to the full nonhomogeneous equation is \(y_p(x) = x^3.\)
    🔗
    Write down the general solution of the nonhomogeneous differential equation.
    🔗
    Hint.
    For a simple real root \(r\text{,}\) the homogeneous solution includes \(e^{rx}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For a complex root \(\alpha \pm \beta i\) of multiplicity \(m\text{,}\) the real homogeneous solutions are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^{\alpha x}\cos(\beta x), \quad e^{\alpha x}\sin(\beta x), \quad x e^{\alpha x}\cos(\beta x), \quad x e^{\alpha x}\sin(\beta x), \quad \ldots, \end{equation*}
    with \(m\) pairs of such terms. Here the repeated complex roots \(-1 \pm 3i\) have multiplicity \(2\text{,}\) so you will have \(4\) real homogeneous terms generated by them.
    🔗
    The general solution of the nonhomogeneous equation is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = y_h(x) + y_p(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Answer.
    The general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y(x) \amp = C_{1} e^{2x} + C_{2} e^{-x}\cos(3x) + C_{3} e^{-x}\sin(3x) \\ \amp + C_{4} x e^{-x}\cos(3x) + C_{5} x e^{-x}\sin(3x) + x^{3}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The given factored characteristic equation for the homogeneous part is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (r - 2)\,\bigl( (r + 1 - 3i)(r + 1 + 3i) \bigr)^{2} = 0. \end{equation*}
    This yields:
    🔗
    • a simple real root \(r = 2\text{,}\) and
      🔗
    • a repeated complex conjugate pair \(-1 \pm 3i\) of multiplicity \(2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    The real solution corresponding to the simple real root is \(e^{2x}.\)
    🔗
    For the repeated complex pair with real part \(-1\) and imaginary part \(3\text{,}\) multiplicity \(2\) produces four real linearly independent solutions:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^{-x}\cos(3x), \qquad e^{-x}\sin(3x), \qquad x e^{-x}\cos(3x), \qquad x e^{-x}\sin(3x). \end{equation*}
    Thus the homogeneous solution is
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y_h(x) \amp = C_{1} e^{2x} + C_{2} e^{-x}\cos(3x) + C_{3} e^{-x}\sin(3x) \\ \amp + C_{4} x e^{-x}\cos(3x) + C_{5} x e^{-x}\sin(3x) \end{align*}
    We are given a particular solution to the full nonhomogeneous equation to be \(y_p(x) = x^{3}.\)
    🔗
    So the general solution of the nonhomogeneous differential equation is
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y(x) \amp = C_{1} e^{2x} + C_{2} e^{-x}\cos(3x) + C_{3} e^{-x}\sin(3x) \\ \amp + C_{4} x e^{-x}\cos(3x) + C_{5} x e^{-x}\sin(3x) + x^{3}. \end{align*}

    6.
    A mass–spring system has mass \(m = 4\) kg and spring constant \(k = 25\) N/m. The motion of the mass satisfies the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 4x'' + 25x = 0. \end{equation*}
    Suppose the mass is released from rest at a displacement of \(x(0) = 2\) meters, so that \(x'(0) = 0\text{.}\) Find the equation of motion \(x(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Divide the equation by \(4\) to obtain the standard form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'' + \omega^{2} x = 0. \end{equation*}
    Here \(\omega = \sqrt{k/m}\text{.}\) The general solution of \(x'' + \omega^{2}x = 0\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = C_1 \cos(\omega t) + C_2 \sin(\omega t). \end{equation*}
    Apply the initial conditions \(x(0)=2\) and \(x'(0)=0\) to find \(C_1\) and \(C_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The angular frequency is \(\omega = 5/2\text{,}\) and the equation of motion is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{5}{2} t\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    We begin with the equation of motion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 4x'' + 25x = 0. \end{equation*}
    Divide through by \(4\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'' + \frac{25}{4} x = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \omega^{2} = \frac{25}{4}, \qquad \omega = \frac{5}{2}. \end{equation*}
    The general solution of \(x'' + \omega^{2}x = 0\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = C_1 \cos\!\left(\frac{5}{2} t\right) + C_2 \sin\!\left(\frac{5}{2} t\right). \end{equation*}
    Apply the initial condition \(x(0) = 2\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(0) = C_1 = 2. \end{equation*}
    Now compute the derivative:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'(t) = - C_1 \frac{5}{2} \sin\!\left(\frac{5}{2} t\right) + C_2 \frac{5}{2} \cos\!\left(\frac{5}{2} t\right). \end{equation*}
    Apply \(x'(0)=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'(0) = C_2 \frac{5}{2} = 0, \qquad \text{so } C_2 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the equation of motion is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{5}{2} t\right). \end{equation*}

    7.
    A mass–spring–damper system is governed by the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 4x'' + 12x' + 9x = 0. \end{equation*}
    Classify the motion of the system as underdamped, critically damped, or overdamped.
    🔗
    Hint.
    Consider the characteristic equation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r^{2} + 3r + \tfrac{9}{4} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Compute the discriminant \(\Delta = b^{2} - 4ac.\)
    🔗
    • If \(\Delta > 0\text{:}\) overdamped.
      🔗
    • If \(\Delta = 0\text{:}\) critically damped.
      🔗
    • If \(\Delta < 0\text{:}\) underdamped.
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The motion is critically damped.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Begin with the equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 4x'' + 12x' + 9x = 0. \end{equation*}
    Divide by \(4\) to obtain the standard form:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'' + 3x' + \tfrac{9}{4}x = 0. \end{equation*}
    The corresponding characteristic equation is \(r^{2} + 3r + \tfrac{9}{4} = 0.\)
    🔗
    Compute the discriminant:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \Delta = 3^{2} - 4\cdot 1 \cdot \tfrac{9}{4} = 9 - 9 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Since the discriminant is zero, the equation has a repeated real root. Therefore the motion is critically damped.

    8.
    Consider the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 3y' + 2y = e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    1. Find the general solution \(y_h\) of the associated homogeneous equation.
      🔗
    2. Use the method of undetermined coefficients to find a particular solution \(y_p\) to the nonhomogeneous equation.
      🔗
    3. Write the general solution \(y = y_h + y_p\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    Hint.
    For part (a), solve the characteristic equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0. \end{equation*}
    For part (b), note that the forcing term is \(e^{x}\text{.}\) Check whether \(e^{x}\) is a solution of the homogeneous equation. If it is not, try a particular solution of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = A e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute this guess into the left-hand side to solve for \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(y_h = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x}\text{.}\) A particular solution is \(y_p = -xe^{x}\text{.}\) The general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x} - xe^{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) Solve the homogeneous equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0. \end{equation*}
    Its characteristic equation is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0. \end{equation*}
    This factors as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (r - 1)(r - 2) = 0, \end{equation*}
    giving roots \(r = 1\) and \(r = 2\text{.}\) Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_h = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    (b) To find a particular solution, try the form \(y_p = A e^{x}\text{.}\) Compute derivatives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = A e^{x}, \qquad y_p'' = A e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute into the differential equation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (A e^{x}) - 3(A e^{x}) + 2(A e^{x}) = e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    The left-hand side simplifies to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 \cdot A e^{x} = e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Since this yields \(0 = 1\text{,}\) our guess fails because \(e^{x}\) is already a solution of the homogeneous equation.
    🔗
    Therefore we multiply our guess by \(x\) and instead try
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = A x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Compute derivatives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = A e^{x} + A x e^{x}, \qquad y_p'' = A e^{x} + A e^{x} + A x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute into the left-hand side:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p'' - 3 y_p' + 2 y_p = (2A e^{x} + A x e^{x}) - 3(A e^{x} + A x e^{x}) + 2(A x e^{x}). \end{equation*}
    Simplify:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} = (2A - 3A)e^{x} + (A - 3A + 2A)x e^{x} = -A e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Set this equal to the forcing term \(e^{x}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} -A e^{x} = e^{x} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad A = -1. \end{equation*}
    Thus a particular solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = -x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    (c) The general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = y_h + y_p = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x} - x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    9.
    Consider the nonhomogeneous differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - y = e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    1. Find the general solution \(y_h\) to the corresponding homogeneous equation.
      🔗
    2. Use variation of parameters to find a particular solution \(y_p\) of the nonhomogeneous equation.
      🔗
    3. Write the general solution \(y = y_h + y_p\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    Hint.
    For the homogeneous equation \(y'' - y = 0\text{,}\) the characteristic equation is \(r^2 - 1 = 0,\) with roots \(r = 1\) and \(r = -1\text{.}\) Thus a fundamental set of solutions is \(y_1 = e^{x}\) and \(y_2 = e^{-x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For variation of parameters, use the formulas
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2 f(x)}{W} \, dx \;+\; y_2 \int \frac{y_1 f(x)}{W} \, dx, \end{equation*}
    where \(W\) is the Wronskian and \(f(x) = e^{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    A homogeneous solution is
    \begin{equation*} y_h = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{-x}. \end{equation*}
    A particular solution is
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \tfrac{1}{2} x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Thus the general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{-x} + \tfrac{1}{2} x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) Solve the homogeneous equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'' - y = 0. \end{equation*}
    The characteristic equation is \(r^2 - 1 = 0,\) which factors as \((r-1)(r+1)=0\text{,}\) giving \(r=1\) and \(r=-1\text{.}\) Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_h = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{-x}. \end{equation*}
    (b) Apply variation of parameters. Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_1 = e^{x}, \qquad y_2 = e^{-x}. \end{equation*}
    Compute the Wronskian:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} W = \begin{vmatrix} e^{x} \amp e^{-x} \\ e^{x} \amp -e^{-x} \end{vmatrix} = -e^{x} e^{-x} - e^{x} e^{-x} = -2. \end{equation*}
    The forcing function is \(f(x) = e^{x}\text{.}\) Use the formula
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2 f(x)}{W} \, dx + y_2 \int \frac{y_1 f(x)}{W} \, dx. \end{equation*}
    Compute each integral:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{y_2 f(x)}{W} = \frac{e^{-x} e^{x}}{-2} = -\tfrac{1}{2}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -y_1 \int \left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\right) \, dx = -e^{x} \left(-\tfrac{1}{2} x\right) = \tfrac{1}{2} x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Next,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{y_1 f(x)}{W} = \frac{e^{x} e^{x}}{-2} = -\tfrac{1}{2} e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    Compute the integral:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_2 \int \left(-\tfrac{1}{2} e^{2x}\right) dx = e^{-x} \left( -\tfrac{1}{4} e^{2x} \right) = -\tfrac{1}{4} e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Combine both contributions:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \tfrac{1}{2} x e^{x} - \tfrac{1}{4} e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    Since a particular solution is needed, we can absorb constant multiples of \(e^{x}\) into the homogeneous term. Thus we may take the simpler equivalent form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \tfrac{1}{2} x e^{x}. \end{equation*}
    (c) The general solution of the nonhomogeneous differential equation is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{-x} + \tfrac{1}{2} x e^{x}. \end{equation*}

    Handout Daily Prep 5.9 - Nonhomogeneous Systems
    

    Section Overview

    We now develop methods for solving nonhomogeneous linear systems of the form \(\vec{x}' = A \vec{x} + \vec{f}(t)\text{.}\) Building on the matrix exponential from earlier, the section introduces the variation‚Äëof‚Äëparameters formula, which expresses the general solution as the sum of a homogeneous solution \(e^{tA}\vec{c}\) and a particular solution obtained through an integral involving left‚Äëmultiplication by \(e^{(t-s)A}\text{.}\) Specifically, the solution is \(\vec{x}(t) = e^{tA}\vec{x}(0) + \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\,ds\text{.}\) The section emphasizes that the exponential acts on the nonhomogeneous forcing by ‚Äúpropagating‚Äù its influence forward through time, mirroring the convolution structure familiar from single‚Äëequation ODEs. Several examples‚Äîespecially involving exponential, polynomial, and sinusoidal forcing‚Äîdemonstrate how this integral formula provides a systematic approach even when guessing a particular solution would be difficult or impossible.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Identifying a Nonhomogeneous System.
    Which of the following is a nonhomogeneous linear system?
    üîó
    • \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x} + \vec{f}(t)\)
    • Correct. A nonhomogeneous system includes an external forcing term \(\vec{f}(t)\text{.}\)
    • \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x}\)
    • No forcing term is present‚Äîthis is the homogeneous case.
    • \(\vec{x}' = \vec{f}(t)\)
    • No‚Äîthis system has no matrix term and is not linear in the usual sense of Section¬†7.9.
    üîó
    2. General Solution Formula.
    What is the general solution to the nonhomogeneous system \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x} + \vec{f}(t)\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = e^{tA}\vec{x}(0) + \int_{0}^{t} e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\,ds\)
    • Correct. This is the variation‚Äëof‚Äëparameters formula.
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = e^{tA}\vec{x}(0) + \vec{f}(t)\)
    • No‚Äîthe forcing term must be integrated, not simply added.
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = e^{tA}\vec{f}(t)\)
    • No‚Äîthis does not solve the differential equation.
    üîó
    3. Meaning of \(e^{(t-s)A}\) in the Integral.
    In the variation‚Äëof‚Äëparameters integral \(\displaystyle \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\,ds\text{,}\) what does \(e^{(t-s)A}\) represent?
    üîó
    • The propagation of the forcing \(\vec{f}(s)\) forward to time \(t\text{.}\)
    • Correct. The exponential transports the effect of the forcing from the earlier time \(s\) to the current time \(t\text{.}\)
    • The derivative of \(\vec{f}(s)\text{.}\)
    • No‚Äînothing in the integral computes a derivative of the forcing.
    • An antiderivative of \(A\text{.}\)
    • No‚Äî\(e^{(t-s)A}\) is the matrix exponential of \(A\text{.}\)
    üîó
    4. Simple Computation in the Diagonal Case.
    Suppose \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{f}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{t} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.\) Which expression correctly represents the integrand \(e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\text{?}\)
    üîó
    • \(\begin{bmatrix} e^{2(t-s)} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp e^{-(t-s)} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} e^{s} \\[3pt] 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
    • Correct. For diagonal \(A\text{,}\) the exponential is diagonal, and the integrand is a product of this exponential and \(\vec{f}(s)\text{.}\)
    • \(\begin{bmatrix} e^{2t} \\ e^{-t} \end{bmatrix}\)
    • No‚Äîthis ignores the dependence on \(s\) and the forcing vector.
    • \(e^{tA}\vec{f}(t)\)
    • No‚Äîthe integral requires \(e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\text{,}\) not evaluation at \(t\text{.}\)
    üîó
    5. Solving an IVP Conceptually.
    To solve the initial value problem \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x} + \vec{f}(t), \vec{x}(0) = \vec{b},\) which of the following steps is correct?
    üîó
    • Compute \(\vec{x}(t) = e^{tA}\vec{b} + \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\,ds\text{.}\)
    • Correct. This is precisely the initial‚Äëvalue formula.
    • Solve only the homogeneous system and ignore \(\vec{f}(t)\text{.}\)
    • No‚Äîthis omits the contribution of the forcing term.
    • Integrate \(\vec{f}(t)\) alone and multiply by \(A\text{.}\)
    • No‚Äîthis does not account for the system dynamics.
    üîó
    6. Solving a Nonhomogeneous System with Diagonal A.
    Consider the system \(\vec{x}' = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \vec{x} + \begin{bmatrix} e^{-t} \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{x}(0) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}.\) Use the variation-of-parameters formula to find an explicit expression for \(\vec{x}(t)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    The solution of \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x} + \vec{f}(t)\) with \(\vec{x}(0)=\vec{b}\) is
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = e^{tA} \vec{b} + \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A} \vec{f}(s)\,ds. \end{equation*}
    For the diagonal matrix \(A\text{,}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{tA} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{2t} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp e^{-t} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{(t-s)A} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{2(t-s)} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp e^{-(t-s)} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    The homogeneous part is:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{tA}\vec{x}(0) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{2t} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    The particular part is:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^t \begin{bmatrix} e^{2(t-s)} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp e^{-(t-s)} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} e^{-s} \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} ds = \int_0^t \begin{bmatrix} e^{2t - 3s} \\ 3 e^{-(t-s)} \end{bmatrix} ds. \end{equation*}
    Compute the integrals:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^t e^{2t - 3s}\,ds = e^{2t}\int_0^t e^{-3s}\,ds = e^{2t} \frac{1 - e^{-3t}}{3}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^t 3e^{-(t-s)}\,ds = 3e^{-t} \int_0^t e^{s}\,ds = 3e^{-t}(e^{t}-1) = 3(1 - e^{-t}). \end{equation*}
    Putting everything together:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{2t} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{3} e^{2t}(1 - e^{-3t}) \\ 3(1-e^{-t}) \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Hence the solution is
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{2t} + \tfrac{1}{3}(e^{2t} - e^{-t}) \\ 3 - 3e^{-t} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    7. Constant Forcing with Triangular Matrix.
    Solve the system \(\vec{x}' = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\vec{x} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ e^{t} \end{bmatrix}, \vec{x}(0) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}.\) Use variation of parameters; do not use undetermined coefficients.
    üîó
    Solution.
    The matrix exponential for an upper triangular matrix with repeated eigenvalue \(1\) is:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{tA} = e^{t} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp t \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Consider the formula
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = e^{tA}\vec{x}(0) + \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\,ds. \end{equation*}
    Compute the homogeneous part:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{tA}\vec{x}(0) = e^{t} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp t \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = e^{t} \begin{bmatrix} 2 - t \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Next compute the integrand:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s) = e^{t-s} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp (t-s) \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ e^{s} \end{bmatrix} = e^{t-s} \begin{bmatrix} (t-s)e^{s} \\ e^{s} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (t-s)e^{t} \\ e^{t} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Integrate from \(0\) to \(t\text{:}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^t (t-s)e^{t}\,ds = e^{t}\int_0^t (t-s)\,ds = e^{t}\left(\tfrac12 t^2\right), \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^t e^{t}\,ds = e^{t} t. \end{equation*}
    Thus the particular solution is:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac12 t^2 e^{t} \\ t e^{t} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Combining homogeneous and particular parts:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = e^{t} \begin{bmatrix} 2 - t \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac12 t^2 e^{t} \\ t e^{t} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (2 - t + \tfrac12 t^2)e^{t} \\ (-1 + t)e^{t} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    8. Interpreting the Forcing Term Through the Matrix Exponential.
    Consider the nonhomogeneous system \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x} + \vec{f}(t), A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix},\) where \(\vec{f}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \cos t \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    1. Describe qualitatively how the forcing term influences the motion using the matrix exponential \(e^{(t-s)A}\text{.}\)
      üîó
      üîó
    2. Compute \(e^{tA}\text{.}\)
      üîó
      üîó
    Solution.
    (a) The forcing enters through the integral
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\vec{f}(s)\,ds, \end{equation*}
    where \(e^{(t-s)A}\) ‚Äúpropagates‚Äù the contribution of \(\vec{f}(s)\) forward from time \(s\) to \(t\text{.}\) Since \(A\) is a rotation matrix, \(e^{(t-s)A}\) is a rotation by \(t-s\text{.}\) Thus the forcing term \(\vec{f}(s)\) (a vertical oscillation) gets rotated and accumulated over time. The net effect is that the system experiences a continuously rotating contribution from \(\cos s\text{,}\) producing a driven rotational motion.
    üîó
    (b) Because \(A\) represents a 90-degree rotation, its exponential is the usual rotation matrix:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} e^{tA} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos t \amp -\sin t \\ \sin t \amp \cos t \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    This follows from the Taylor series expansion and is used directly inside the nonhomogeneous integral.
    üîó
    üîó

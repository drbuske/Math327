    Handout Daily Prep 5.4 - Eigenvalue Method
    

    Section Overview

    We now introduce the eigenvalue method as a powerful technique for solving linear homogeneous constantâ€‘coefficient systems of ODEs of the form \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{,}\) where \(P\) is a constant square matrix. Motivated by the scalar case, the method tries solutions of the form \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) where \(\lambda\) is a scalar and \(\vec{v}\) is a constant vector. Substituting this trial form into the system reduces the differential equation to the algebraic eigenvalue problem \(P\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}\text{.}\) Thus, eigenvalues are found by solving \(\det(P - \lambda I) = 0\text{,}\) and each eigenvector yields a corresponding solution \(\vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\text{.}\) Since an \(n \times n\) matrix typically has \(n\) eigenvalues (possibly repeated or complex), the section shows that a collection of linearly independent eigenvector solutions forms a fundamental solution set, and all solutions are linear combinations of these. The method connects linear algebra and differential equations, enabling systematic and elegant solution of multiâ€‘dimensional linear systems.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Trial Solutions for the Eigenvalue Method.
    For a linear homogeneous constantâ€‘coefficient system \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{,}\) what trial solution does the eigenvalue method use?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\)
    • Correct. The method assumes a solution of the form \(\vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\text{.}\)
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = \lambda t + \vec{v}\)
    • Noâ€”the method uses exponential solutions, not linear ones.
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v}\,\sin(\lambda t)\)
    • Not in the first step. Sinusoidal behavior only appears later when eigenvalues are complex.
    ðŸ”—
    2. Obtaining the Eigenvalue Equation.
    After substituting \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\) into \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{,}\) which equation must the constants \(\lambda\) and \(\vec{v}\) satisfy?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(P\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}\)
    • Correct. The substitution leads directly to the eigenvalue equation.
    • \(P\vec{v} = \vec{0}\)
    • Noâ€”that equation is used to find eigenvectors once \(\lambda\) is known.
    • \(P = \lambda I\)
    • No. This only holds when \(P\) is a scalar matrix.
    ðŸ”—
    3. Computing Eigenvalues.
    To find the eigenvalues of a matrix \(P\) in the system \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{,}\) which equation should be solved?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(\det(P - \lambda I) = 0\)
    • Correct. Eigenvalues are roots of the characteristic equation \(\det(P - \lambda I)=0\text{.}\)
    • \(\det(P) = 0\)
    • No. This would test whether \(P\) is singular, not find eigenvalues.
    • \(P\vec{x} = \vec{0}\)
    • This computes the nullspace, not the eigenvalues.
    ðŸ”—
    4. Computing an Eigenvalue.
    Let \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}.\) What are the eigenvalues of \(P\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    • \(\lambda = 3,\ 5\)
    • Correct. Diagonal matrices have eigenvalues equal to their diagonal entries, consistent with solving \(\det(P - \lambda I)=0\text{.}\)
    • \(\lambda = 0,\ 15\)
    • Noâ€”the determinant equation does not give these roots.
    • \(\lambda = 4\) only
    • No. A 2Ã—2 matrix must have two eigenvalues (including repeats).
    ðŸ”—
    5. Checking an Eigenvector.
    Let \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}.\) Compute \(P\vec{v}\) and determine the corresponding eigenvalue.
    ðŸ”—
    • \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \lambda = 2\)
    • Correct. Because \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 0\end{bmatrix} = 2\vec{v}\text{,}\) the eigenvalue is \(\lambda = 2\text{.}\) This matches the equation \(P\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}\) used in the eigenvalue method.
    • \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \lambda = 1\)
    • No. This incorrectly multiplies the matrix and vector.
    • \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \lambda = 2\)
    • No. The second component should remain zero when multiplying by this matrix.
    ðŸ”—
    6. Checking Whether a Vector is an Eigenvector.
    Let \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}.\) Compute \(P\vec{v}\) and determine whether \(\vec{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(P\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    • \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix},\) so \(\vec{v}\) is not an eigenvector.
    • Correct. Because \(P\vec{v} = (4,\,3)^{T}\) is not a scalar multiple of \((1,\,3)^{T}\text{,}\) \(\vec{v}\) is not an eigenvector. Eigenvectors must satisfy \(P\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}\text{.}\)
    • \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix},\) so \(\vec{v}\) is an eigenvector with \(\lambda = 4\text{.}\)
    • No. \((4,3)^{T}\) is not equal to \(4(1,3)^{T} = (4,12)^{T}\text{.}\)
    • \(P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) so \(\vec{v}\) is an eigenvector with \(\lambda = 1\text{.}\)
    • Noâ€”the computation of \(P\vec{v}\) is incorrect.
    ðŸ”—
    7. Verifying a Solution from an Eigenpair.
    Suppose \(\lambda = 3\) is an eigenvalue of a constant matrix \(P\) with eigenvector \(\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}.\) Which of the following is a solution of the system \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} e^{3t}\)
    • Correct. Every eigenpair \((\lambda,\vec{v})\) yields a solution \(\vec{x}(t)=\vec{v}e^{\lambda t}\text{.}\)
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} e^{t}\)
    • No. This uses \(e^{t}\) instead of \(e^{3t}\text{.}\)
    • \(\vec{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} e^{t}\)
    • No. Even though the vector is a scalar multiple of \(\vec{v}\text{,}\) the exponential must be \(e^{3t}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    8. Deriving the Eigenvalue Equation.
    Consider a linear homogeneous constant-coefficient system \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{.}\) Starting from the trial solution \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) derive the algebraic eigenvalue equation that \(\lambda\) and \(\vec{v}\) must satisfy. Explain each step clearly.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The trial solution used is \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) where \(\lambda\) is a scalar and \(\vec{v}\) is a constant vector. We substitute this into \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    First compute the derivative:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}'(t) = \lambda \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute both expressions into the differential equation:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \lambda \vec{v} e^{\lambda t} = P(\vec{v} e^{\lambda t}) = (P\vec{v}) e^{\lambda t}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(e^{\lambda t}\) is never zero, canceling it yields the purely algebraic condition:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \lambda \vec{v} = P\vec{v}. \end{equation*}
    This is exactly the eigenvalue equation. It states that \(\vec{v}\) must be an eigenvector of \(P\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\) The section emphasizes this derivation as the foundation of the eigenvalue method.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    9. Verifying an Eigenvector Solution.
    Let \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \lambda = 3.\) Verify that \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\) is a solution of the system \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We must check whether \(\vec{x}'(t) = P\vec{x}(t)\text{.}\) Start by computing \(P\vec{v}\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} P\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot 2 + 2\cdot 1 \\ 0\cdot 2 + 3\cdot 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Compute \(\lambda \vec{v}\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \lambda \vec{v} = 3 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(P\vec{v} \neq \lambda \vec{v}\text{,}\) the vector \(\vec{v}\) is not an eigenvector of \(P\text{,}\) and therefore \(\vec{x}(t) = \vec{v} e^{3t}\) is not a solution.
    ðŸ”—
    This exercise illustrates a key requirement: a solution of the form \(\vec{v} e^{\lambda t}\) exists only when \(P\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}\) holds.

    ðŸ”—

    10. Constructing a General Solution from Two Eigenpairs.
    Suppose a system \(\vec{x}' = P\vec{x}\) has two distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = 4\) with corresponding eigenvectors \(\vec{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.\) Write down the general solution to the system and briefly explain why these two solutions form a fundamental set.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since each eigenpair \((\lambda_i, \vec{v}_i)\) gives a solution \(\vec{x}_i(t) = \vec{v}_i e^{\lambda_i t}\text{,}\) we have two independent solutions:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}_1(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} e^{t}, \vec{x}_2(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} e^{4t}. \end{equation*}
    The general solution is the linear combination:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = C_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} e^{t} + C_2 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} e^{4t}. \end{equation*}
    Because the eigenvalues \(1\) and \(4\) are distinct, the corresponding eigenvectors are automatically linearly independent. Hence these two solutions form a fundamental set, and every solution to the system is their linear combination, exactly as described in the theory.

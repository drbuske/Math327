    Handout Daily Prep 1.6 - Autonomous Equations
    

    Section Overview

    This section introduces autonomous differential equations, equations of the form \(x' = f(x)\) whose rate of change depends only on the dependent variable and not on the indpendent variable (which is often time \(t\)). Because of this simplification, autonomous equations allow powerful qualitative analysis: identifying equilibrium (constant) solutions, determining critical points where \(f(x)=0\text{,}\) and analyzing the stability of these equilibria by observing how nearby solutions behave. Using examples such as Newton’s law of cooling and the logistic population model, the section shows how slope fields reveal long‑term behavior—such as convergence to a stable equilibrium or divergence from an unstable one—even without solving the ODE explicitly.
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Exercises

    1.
    In Section 1.6, you read the following sentence:
    🔗
    \(x = A\) is “stable” in that small perturbations in \(x\) do not lead to substantially different solutions as \(t\) grows.
    🔗
    🔗
    What is meant by the word perturbation in this context?
    🔗
    Solution.
    In this context, a perturbation means a small change in the initial value of the solution. That is, instead of starting exactly at the equilibrium value \(x(0)=A\text{,}\) we begin with a nearby initial condition such as \(x(0)=A+\varepsilon\) for some small number \(\varepsilon\text{.}\) The question is how this small deviation affects the long‑term behavior of the solution curve.
    🔗
    When an equilibrium \(x=A\) is described as “stable,” it means that these small perturbations—small changes to the starting value—do not lead to solutions that drift far away from \(A\) as \(t\) increases. The nearby solutions remain close to the equilibrium curve, reflecting the intuitive idea that the system “pulls” the solution back toward
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2. Identifying an Autonomous Equation.
    Which of the following differential equations is autonomous?
    🔗
    • \(\displaystyle y' = y(3 - y)\)
    • Correct. An autonomous equation has the form \(y' = f(y)\text{,}\) depending only on \(y\text{.}\)
    • \(y' = x + y\)
    • This depends explicitly on \(x\text{,}\) so it is not autonomous.
    • \(y' = e^{x} y^{2}\)
    • Not autonomous because of the \(e^{x}\) factor.
    🔗
    3. Equilibrium Solutions.
    For the autonomous equation \(y' = y(2-y)\text{,}\) which of the following are equilibrium solutions?
    🔗
    • \(y = 0\) and \(y = 2\)
    • Correct. Equilibria occur where \(f(y)=0\text{,}\) so \(y(2-y)=0\) gives \(y=0,2\text{.}\)
    • \(y = 1\)
    • At \(y=1\text{,}\) \(y'=1(1)=1\text{,}\) so it is not an equilibrium.
    • No equilibrium solutions
    • Incorrect—two exist.
    🔗
    4. Stability From the Sign of \(f'(y)\).
    Consider \(y' = y(3-y)\text{.}\) Determine the stability of the equilibrium \(y = 3\text{.}\) Use the sign of \(f'(y)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • Stable, because \(f'(3) = -3\) is negative.
    • Correct. For autonomous equations, an equilibrium \(y=A\) is stable if \(f'(A)<0\text{.}\) Here \(f(y)=y(3-y)\) gives \(f'(y)=3-2y\text{,}\) so \(f'(3)=-3<0\text{.}\)
    • Unstable, because \(f'(3) = 3\)
    • No—this computes the derivative incorrectly.
    • Neither stable nor unstable
    • Incorrect—its stability is determined by \(f'(3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    5. Solving a Simple Autonomous ODE.
    Solve the IVP \(y' = 2y\text{,}\) \(y(0)=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • \(y = 5e^{2x}\)
    • Correct. Integrate \(y'/y = 2\) to get \(y=Ce^{2x}\text{,}\) then apply \(y(0)=5\text{.}\)
    • \(y = 5e^{x/2}\)
    • Incorrect exponent.
    • \(y = 2e^{5x}\)
    • Incorrect constant and exponent.
    🔗
    6. Interpreting a Phase Line.
    For an autonomous equation \(y' = f(y)\text{,}\) suppose the phase line shows arrows pointing toward \(y=1\) from both above and below. What does this indicate?
    🔗
    • \(y = 1\) is a stable equilibrium.
    • Correct. When trajectories move toward an equilibrium from both sides, that equilibrium is stable.
    • \(y = 1\) is an unstable equilibrium.
    • No—unstable equilibria repel nearby solutions.
    • There is no equilibrium at \(y=1\text{.}\)
    • No—the phase line arrows imply an equilibrium there.
    🔗
    7.
    How does the solution to the autonomous IVP \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\sin(y),\; y(-3)=1\) compare to the solution of the IVP \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\sin(y),\; y(1)=1\text{?}\) Can you generalize this idea for any autonomous equation? A slope field for \(y'=\sin(y)\) is given in Figure 10.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Because the equation is autonomous (the slope depends only on \(y\) and not on \(x\)), the two solutions differ only by a horizontal shift. Both IVPs start with \(y=1\text{,}\) and all solution curves through \(y=1\) follow exactly the same shape in the slope field. The only difference is where on the \(x\)-axis the solution meets that level.
    🔗
    In general, for any autonomous equation \(y'=f(y)\text{,}\) if two initial values share the same \(y\)-coordinate but occur at different \(x\)-values, then the corresponding solutions are identical up to horizontal translation. This is because the slope at each point depends only on \(y\text{,}\) so moving left or right simply shifts the solution curve without changing its shape.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    Values of \(y_0\) for which \(f(y_0)=0\) are called critical points. They provide equilibrium solutions of the autonomous equation \(\frac{dy}{dx}=f(y)\text{,}\) i.e. \(y(x)\equiv y_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Why are equilibrium solutions constant solutions? (Hint: Remember the result of the previous problem.)
    🔗
    Solution.
    If \(f(y_0)=0\text{,}\) then substituting \(y(x)\equiv y_0\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(x) = f(y_0) = 0. \end{equation*}
    This differential equation says the slope is zero for all \(x\text{,}\) so the function must remain constant. Geometrically (as in the previous problem), the slope field has horizontal segments at that \(y\)-value, and any solution starting there stays on that horizontal line for all time. Thus equilibrium solutions are constant solutions.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    1. Determine the equilibrium solutions to \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = y^{2} - y - 2.\) Use the slope field shown in Figure 11 to confirm your finding.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Solution curves often approach or move away from equilibrium solutions as \(x\) increases; this is called stability.
      🔗
      1. If nearby solutions approach \(y_0\text{,}\) the equilibrium is a sink (stable).
        🔗
        🔗
      2. If some nearby solutions do not approach \(y_0\text{,}\) the equilibrium is unstable.
        🔗
        🔗
      3. If all nearby solutions move away, the equilibrium is a source (unstable).
        🔗
        🔗
      Determine the stability of the two equilibrium solutions \(y_1(x)\equiv -1\) and \(y_2(x)\equiv 2\) for \(\displaystyle y'=y^2 - y -2.\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Solve \(y^{2}-y-2=0\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} (y-2)(y+1)=0. \end{equation*}
      Thus the equilibrium solutions are \(y=-1\) and \(y=2\text{.}\) In the slope field, horizontal segments occur at exactly these values.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Stability analysis.
      🔗
      Let \(f(y)=y^{2}-y-2\text{.}\) Then \(f'(y)=2y-1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      • At \(y=-1\text{:}\)
        🔗
        \begin{equation*} f'(-1) = -2 - 1 = -3 < 0. \end{equation*}
        Negative derivative means nearby solutions move toward \(-1\text{;}\) thus \(y=-1\) is a stable equilibrium (sink).
        🔗
        🔗
      • At \(y=2\text{:}\)
        🔗
        \begin{equation*} f'(2) = 4 - 1 = 3 > 0. \end{equation*}
        Positive derivative means nearby solutions move away from \(2\text{;}\) thus \(y=2\) is an unstable equilibrium (source).
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    10.
    A disease is spreading through a country. Let \(x\) be the number of infected people, and \(S\) the number of susceptible people. The infection rate \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) is proportional to the product of the number of infected people \(x\) and the number of susceptible people \(S - x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Write down the differential equation.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Suppose \(x(0)>0\text{.}\) Compute \(\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Does this result agree with your intuition? Explain.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. The rate is proportional to \(x(S-x)\text{,}\) so the model is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = k\, x(S-x), \end{equation*}
      where \(k>0\) is the infection constant.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. This logistic‑type equation has equilibria at \(x=0\) and \(x=S\text{.}\) For \(x(0)>0\text{,}\) the solution increases and approaches the carrying capacity \(S\text{.}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t) = S. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Yes—this matches intuition. As long as some people are initially infected, eventually everyone susceptible becomes infected in this simplified model, so the number of infected tends toward \(S\text{.}\) Real disease dynamics can be more complicated, but in this autonomous model the infection saturates the susceptible population.
      🔗
      🔗

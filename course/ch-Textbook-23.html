    Handout Daily Prep 5.2 - Matrices and Linear Systems
    

    Section Overview

    We now introduce matrices as essential tools for studying linear systems of ODEs. The section reviews the basic structure of a matrix as an \(m \times n\) array, defines vectors as column matrices, and recalls fundamental matrix operations such as scalar multiplication, addition, and transpose. With this foundation, the section develops matrix multiplication using the dot product and emphasizes the importance of compatible dimensions (e.g., multiplying an \(m \times n\) matrix by an \(n \times p\) matrix to obtain an \(m \times p\) matrix). These operations enable us to rewrite a system of linear ODEs concisely as a single vector equation \(\vec{x}' = A \vec{x}\text{,}\) preparing the way for later methods such as the eigenvalue approach and matrix exponentials. The goal is to transform systems into a compact algebraic form that reveals structure and makes computations more systematic.

    1. Matrix Size and Vectors.
    Which of the following correctly describes a column vector?
    ðŸ”—
    • A column vector is an \(m \times 1\) matrix.
    • Correct. A vector is treated as a column matrix in this section.
    • An \(1 \times m\) matrix.
    • Noâ€”that is a row vector.
    • A square matrix with \(m=n\text{.}\)
    • Noâ€”square matrices are unrelated to the definition of vectors.
    ðŸ”—
    2. Matrix Multiplication Rules.
    When is the matrix product \(A B\) defined?
    ðŸ”—
    • When \(A\) is \(m \times n\) and \(B\) is \(n \times p\text{.}\)
    • Correct. The number of columns of \(A\) must equal the number of rows of \(B\text{.}\)
    • When both matrices have the same number of rows.
    • No. Matching rows is not the requirement.
    • Only when both matrices are square.
    • No. Matrix multiplication does not require square matrices.
    ðŸ”—
    3. Writing a System in Matrix Form.
    Suppose we have the system
    \begin{align*} x_1' \amp = 2x_1 + 3x_2, \\ x_2' \amp = -x_1 + 4x_2. \end{align*}
    Which vectorâ€‘matrix form is correct?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(\vec{x}' = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \vec{x}\)
    • Correct. The matrix is constructed from the coefficients of the system exactly as described in the section.
    • \(\vec{x}' = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \amp x_2 \\ x_1 \amp x_2 \end{bmatrix}\)
    • No. The matrix should contain constants, not variables.
    • \(\vec{x}' = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 + 3x_2 \\ -x_1 + 4x_2 \end{bmatrix}\)
    • This is simply rewriting the system, not expressing it as \(A \vec{x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    4. Understanding the Transpose.
    If \(A\) is a matrix, what is the transpose \(A^{T}\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    • The matrix obtained by swapping rows and columns of \(A\text{.}\)
    • Correct. The transpose flips rows into columns and vice versa.
    • The matrix formed by multiplying every entry of \(A\) by \(-1\text{.}\)
    • No. That describes scalar multiplication by \(-1\text{,}\) not transposition.
    • The matrix you obtain by reversing the order of columns only.
    • No. Transpose reverses rows and columns.
    ðŸ”—
    5. Determinant and Invertibility.
    For a square matrix \(A\text{,}\) which statement about its determinant is correct?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(A\) is invertible exactly when \(\det(A) \neq 0\text{.}\)
    • Correct. A nonzero determinant means the matrix is invertible, which is essential when rewriting or solving linear systems using \(\vec{x}' = A\vec{x}\text{.}\)
    • \(\det(A)\) must always be positive for \(A\) to be invertible.
    • No. A determinant may be negative or positive; only zero prevents invertibility.
    • \(A\) is invertible when \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. A zero determinant means \(A\) is singular and cannot be inverted.
    ðŸ”—
    6. Understanding Inverses.
    Suppose \(A\) is a square matrix and \(A^{-1}\) exists. What property does \(A^{-1}\) satisfy?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(A A^{-1} = I\) and \(A^{-1} A = I\text{.}\)
    • Correct. The inverse undoes the effect of \(A\text{,}\) producing the identity matrix \(I\text{.}\)
    • \(A^{-1}\) is obtained by transposing \(A\text{.}\)
    • No. The transpose \(A^{T}\) is generally unrelated to the inverse.
    • \(A^{-1}\) exists for every square matrix.
    • Incorrect. Only matrices with nonzero determinant are invertible.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}, B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}.\) Compute \(2A + B\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    First compute \(2A\) by multiplying every entry of \(A\) by 2:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} 2A = \begin{bmatrix} 2\cdot 1 \amp 2\cdot 2 \\ 2\cdot 3 \amp 2\cdot 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 4 \\ 6 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Now add \(B\) elementâ€‘wise:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} 2A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 4 \\ 6 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 4 \\ 5 \amp 13 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Matrix addition and scalar multiplication work entry by entry.

    8.
    Let \(C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 4 \amp 2 \\ -3 \amp 0 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}.\) Compute the transpose \(C^{T}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The transpose swaps rows and columns. So the first row of \(C\) becomes the first column of \(C^{T}\text{,}\) and the second row becomes the second column.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} C^{T} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -3 \\ 4 \amp 0 \\ 2 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

    9.
    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}, \vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}.\) Compute \(A \vec{x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.

    Second row: \(-1\cdot 1 + 4\cdot 2 = -1 + 8 = 7\text{.}\)

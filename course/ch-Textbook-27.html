    Section Overview

    We now introduce second-order systems of differential equations, with a central focus on physical applications such as undamped massâ€“spring systems. Instead of rewriting these problems as large first-order systems, the section treats them directly in their natural second-order form, where the displacement vector \(\vec{x}\) satisfies an equation of the type \(M \vec{x}'' = K \vec{x}\text{,}\) with \(M\) the mass matrix and \(K\) the stiffness matrix. Using Hookeâ€™s law and Newtonâ€™s second law, the dynamics of several coupled masses can be expressed compactly in matrix notation, revealing structural patterns that generalize to an arbitrary number of masses. Because \(M\) is diagonal and invertible, the system can be rewritten as \(\vec{x}'' = M^{-1} K \vec{x}\text{,}\) which leads to oscillatory behavior governed by the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(M^{-1}K\text{.}\) The section highlights how these ideas connect discrete massâ€“spring models to continuum models such as the wave equation, showing how second-order systems arise naturally in many physical contexts.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Setting Up a Second-Order System.
    In the threeâ€‘mass spring system, which equation correctly represents the mathematical model obtained from Newtonâ€™s second law and Hookeâ€™s law?
    ðŸ”—
    • \(M \vec{x}'' = K \vec{x}\)
    • Correct. The section derives the system \(M \vec{x}'' = K \vec{x}\text{,}\) where \(M\) is the mass matrix and \(K\) is the stiffness matrix.
    • \(M \vec{x}' = K \vec{x}\)
    • Noâ€”this would describe a firstâ€‘order system, not a secondâ€‘order one.
    • \(K \vec{x}'' = M \vec{x}\)
    • Noâ€”Newtonâ€™s law leads to mass times acceleration on the left.
    ðŸ”—
    2. Inverting the Mass Matrix.
    Why is it easy to compute \(M^{-1}\) for the mass matrix \(M\) in these models?
    ðŸ”—
    • Because \(M\) is diagonal with nonzero entries.
    • Correct. Each mass is nonzero, and \(M\) is diagonal, so \(M^{-1}\) is obtained by inverting each diagonal entry.
    • Because \(M = I\) for any massâ€“spring system.
    • No. The diagonal entries are the masses, not ones.
    • Because \(M\) is symmetric.
    • Symmetry alone does not make inversion easy; diagonal form does.
    ðŸ”—
    3. Meaning of \(M^{-1}K\).
    After rewriting the system as \(\vec{x}'' = M^{-1} K \vec{x}\text{,}\) what is the significance of the matrix \(M^{-1}K\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    • Its eigenvalues determine the natural frequencies of the system.
    • Correct. The section explains that the eigenvalues of \(M^{-1}K\) give the oscillation frequencies (normal modes) of the massâ€“spring system.
    • It measures how quickly the masses grow without bound.
    • No. The system models oscillations, not exponential growth.
    • It determines whether \(M\) is invertible.
    • No. \(M\) is invertible because the masses are nonzero.
    ðŸ”—
    4. Computing One Entry of \(M^{-1}K\).
    Consider a twoâ€‘mass system with \(M = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix}, K = \begin{bmatrix} -5 \amp 4 \\ 4 \amp -6 \end{bmatrix}.\) What is the \((1,2)\) entry of \(M^{-1}K\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    • \(2\)
    • Correct. \(M^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp \tfrac{1}{3} \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) so the \((1,2)\) entry is \(\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 4 = 2\text{.}\) This matches how secondâ€‘order systems become \(\vec{x}'' = M^{-1}K \vec{x}\text{.}\)
    • \(4\)
    • Noâ€”this is the \((1,2)\) entry of \(K\text{,}\) not \(M^{-1}K\text{.}\)
    • \(\tfrac{4}{3}\)
    • Noâ€”the second row would use \(\tfrac{1}{3}\text{,}\) not the first.
    ðŸ”—
    5. Interpreting a Computed Eigenvalue.
    Suppose a secondâ€‘order system \(\vec{x}'' = M^{-1}K \vec{x}\) for some twoâ€‘mass model yields an eigenvalue \(\lambda = -9\text{.}\) What does this tell you about the behavior of the mode corresponding to this eigenvalue?
    ðŸ”—
    • The mode oscillates with natural frequency \(3\text{.}\)
    • Correct. For \(\vec{x}'' = \lambda \vec{x}\text{,}\) a negative \(\lambda\) produces oscillations with frequency \(\sqrt{-\lambda} = 3\text{.}\) The section explains how eigenvalues of \(M^{-1}K\) encode natural frequencies.
    • The mode grows exponentially without bound.
    • No. Exponential growth occurs when \(\lambda>0\text{.}\)
    • The mode decays exponentially to zero.
    • No. Negative eigenvalues in this setup produce oscillations, not decay.
    ðŸ”—
    6. Energy Interpretation of a Second-Order System.
    Consider a second-order system of the form \(M \vec{x}'' = K \vec{x}\text{,}\) where \(M\) is the mass matrix and \(K\) is the stiffness matrix of an undamped massâ€“spring system.
    ðŸ”—
    (a) Explain why this system conserves mechanical energy when there is no damping. (b) Describe the physical meaning of the matrices \(M\) and \(K\) in terms of kinetic and potential energy.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    (a) In an undamped massâ€“spring system, no forces remove energy from the system. The springs store potential energy and the masses carry kinetic energy, and energy simply transfers back and forth between these two forms. The equation \(M \vec{x}'' = K \vec{x}\) results from applying Newtonâ€™s second law and Hookeâ€™s law to the system, and because no damping forces are present, the total mechanical energy
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} E(t) = \tfrac12 (\vec{x}')^{\!T} M \vec{x}' + \tfrac12 \vec{x}^{T} K \vec{x} \end{equation*}
    remains constant. This conservation reflects exactly the behavior of the undamped multi-mass system.
    ðŸ”—
    (b) The matrix \(M\) represents the distribution of inertia in the system. Because it is diagonal with entries \(m_i\text{,}\) the kinetic energy term
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \tfrac12 (\vec{x}')^{\!T} M \vec{x}' \end{equation*}
    is exactly \(\tfrac12 ( m_1 x_1'^2 + m_2 x_2'^2 + \cdots )\text{.}\) The stiffness matrix \(K\) encodes how the springs resist displacement. The potential energy stored in the system is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \tfrac12 \vec{x}^{T} K \vec{x}. \end{equation*}
    Off-diagonal entries of \(K\) represent coupling between masses (neighboring displacements affect each other), while diagonal entries reflect how strongly the system resists stretching at each mass location. This matches the physical interpretation provided alongside the construction of \(M\) and \(K\) in the text.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    7. Understanding Normal Modes Without Calculation.
    Suppose a three-mass undamped spring system is modeled by \(M \vec{x}'' = K \vec{x}\text{.}\) Let the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(M^{-1}K\) be \((-\omega_1^2, \vec{v}_1)\text{,}\) \((-\omega_2^2, \vec{v}_2)\text{,}\) and \((-\omega_3^2, \vec{v}_3)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Explain, in words and without doing any computation, what the motion looks like when the system is started exactly in the shape of the eigenvector \(\vec{v}_2\) with some initial velocity along \(\vec{v}_2\text{.}\) Why does no other mode appear in the motion?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    If the initial displacement and velocity are both multiples of \(\vec{v}_2\text{,}\) then the system begins in the exact shape of the second normal mode. Each eigenpair produces an independent oscillatory solution \(\vec{v}_i e^{\pm i\omega_i t}\text{,}\) and that the general motion is a linear combination of these normal modes.
    ðŸ”—
    Because \(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \vec{v}_3\) form a basis of independent modes, any displacement can be written as a combination of them. But if the initial state lies entirely in the direction of \(\vec{v}_2\text{,}\) there is no component of \(\vec{v}_1\) or \(\vec{v}_3\) in the initial condition. Therefore the solution must remain a pure oscillation of the form
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}(t) = A \vec{v}_2 \cos(\omega_2 t) + B \vec{v}_2 \sin(\omega_2 t), \end{equation*}
    with frequency \(\omega_2\text{.}\) No other frequency can appear because there is no component of the initial state aligned with the other eigenvectors. This is exactly the decoupling property of normal modes.

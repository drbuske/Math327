    Handout Daily Prep 3.4 - Dirac Delta and Impulse Response
    

    Section Overview

    In this section we explore the idea of modeling instantaneous “impulses” using the Dirac delta, a generalized function that concentrates all its mass at a single point. The section begins by examining short rectangular pulses and their Laplace transforms, then shows how letting the pulse width approach zero leads to the definition of the delta function, an object meaningful only under an integral sign. You will learn how \(\delta (t)\) captures the effect of a sudden input—analogous to striking a system with a hammer—and how its Laplace transform allows us to analyze impulse responses of differential‑equation models.
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Exercises

    1.
    Sometimes the Dirac delta function introduced in Section 3.4 is called a distribution. Which terminology do you prefer, and why?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Mathematically, the Dirac delta cannot be treated as an ordinary function, because it does not have a pointwise value and its defining property \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t)\,\varphi(t)\,dt = \varphi(0)\) holds only when understood in the sense of linear functionals acting on test functions. Calling \(\delta(t)\) a distribution emphasizes this correct mathematical nature.
    🔗
    On the other hand, in engineering, physics, and applied differential equations, the word delta function is often more intuitive: it suggests an “impulse concentrated at a single moment.” When solving ODEs via Laplace transforms, thinking of \(\delta(t)\) as an impulse is often the most natural way to interpret it.
    🔗
    Your personal preference may depend on context:
    • “Distribution” — when emphasizing rigor or explaining why \(\delta(t)\) is not an ordinary function.
      🔗
    • “Delta function” — when working on applied problems, impulse response, or Laplace transforms, where the operational meaning is clear and useful.
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2. Interpretation of the Dirac Delta.
    How is the Dirac delta \(\delta(t)\) interpreted?
    🔗
    • A concentrated unit impulse at \(t=0\) that inputs “1 unit of area” instantaneously.
    • Correct. Section 3.4 treats \(\delta(t)\) as an idealized impulse with total area 1.
    • A function equal to infinity for all \(t\text{.}\)
    • No—it is zero everywhere except at a single instant.
    • An ordinary continuous function.
    • No—the delta is not a classical function; it is a distribution.
    🔗
    3. Laplace Transform of \(\delta(t)\).
    What is the Laplace transform of \(\delta(t)\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \({\cal L}[\delta(t)](s) = 1\)
    • Correct. \({\cal L}[\delta(t)](s) = 1\text{.}\)
    • \({\cal L}[\delta(t)](s) = s\)
    • No—the delta transform is constant, not growing with \(s\text{.}\)
    • \({\cal L}[\delta(t)](s) = e^{-s}\)
    • No—that is the transform of \(\delta(t-1)\text{,}\) not \(\delta(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    4. Transform of a Shifted Delta.
    What is the Laplace transform of the shifted delta \(\delta(t-a)\) for \(a > 0\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \({\cal L}[\delta(t-a)](s) = e^{-as}\)
    • Correct. The delta "picks out" the value at \(t=a\text{,}\) giving \(e^{-as}\text{.}\)
    • \({\cal L}[\delta(t-a)](s) = s e^{-as}\)
    • No—the factor \(s\) does not appear.
    • \({\cal L}[\delta(t-a)](s) = \delta(s-a)\)
    • No—the Laplace transform is not another delta distribution.
    🔗
    5. Meaning of Impulse Response.
    What does the impulse response of a linear ODE represent?
    🔗
    • The solution of the ODE when the input is a delta impulse \(\delta(t)\text{.}\)
    • Correct. The impulse response is the solution when the forcing is \(\delta(t)\text{.}\)
    • The solution for an arbitrary forcing \(f(t)\text{.}\)
    • No—that is the general forced solution, not specifically the impulse response.
    • The steady-state response only.
    • No—the impulse response includes transient behavior as well.
    🔗
    6. Simple Impulse‑Driven ODE.
    Consider the IVP \(x'' + x = \delta(t)\text{,}\) \(x(0)=0\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=0\text{.}\) What is \(X(s)\text{,}\) the Laplace transform of the solution?
    🔗
    • \(X(s) = \dfrac{1}{s^{2}+1}\)
    • Correct. Laplace transforming gives \(s^{2}X(s) + X(s) = 1\text{,}\) hence \(X(s)=1/(s^{2}+1)\text{.}\)
    • \(X(s) = \dfrac{s}{s^{2}+1}\)
    • No—that corresponds to initial velocity, not a delta input.
    • \(X(s)=1\)
    • No—the transform must include the system’s denominator \(s^{2}+1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    7.
    Compute the Laplace transform of \(\delta(t-3)\text{.}\) Then, compute the Laplace transform of \(5\,\delta(t-3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} {\cal L}[\delta(t-a)](s) = e^{-as}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\cal L}[\delta(t-3)](s) = e^{-3s}. \end{equation*}
    Linearity of the Laplace transform gives:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} {\cal L}[5\,\delta(t-3)](s) \amp = 5\,{\cal L}[\delta(t-3)] \\ \amp = 5 e^{-3s}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    Solve the IVP
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x' + 2x = \delta(t), \qquad x(0)=0. \end{equation*}
    (This solution is the impulse response of the system.)
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Take Laplace transforms.
    🔗
    The Laplace transform of \(x'\) is \(sX(s) - x(0) = sX(s)\text{.}\) Also, \({\cal L}[\delta(t)](s) = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} sX(s) + 2X(s) = 1. \end{equation*}
    Solve for \(X(s)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{s+2}. \end{equation*}
    2. Invert the transform.
    🔗
    The inverse Laplace transform of \(1/(s+2)\) is \(x(t)=e^{-2t}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This satisfies the jump condition created by the delta impulse at \(t=0\text{,}\) and it obeys the initial condition \(x(0)=0\) in the appropriate distributional sense.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    Solve the IVP (find the impulse response)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'' + x' + x = \delta(t), \qquad x(0)=0,\; x'(0)=0. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    1. Take the Laplace transform of the ODE.
    🔗
    Using the formulas \({\cal L}\{x''\} = s^{2}X(s) - sx(0) - x'(0)\) and \({\cal L}\{x'\} = sX(s) - x(0)\text{,}\) and given \(x(0)=0\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=0\text{,}\) we obtain:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s^{2}X(s) + sX(s) + X(s) = {\cal L}\{\delta(t)\} = 1. \end{equation*}
    Factor the left-hand side:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (s^{2} + s + 1)X(s) = 1. \end{equation*}
    Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{s^{2} + s + 1}. \end{equation*}
    2. Complete the square.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s^{2} + s + 1 = \left(s + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2} + \tfrac{3}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{\left(s + \tfrac12\right)^{2} + \left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    3. Use the standard inverse Laplace transform
    🔗
    The formula \({\cal L}^{-1}\!\left\{ \dfrac{1}{(s+a)^{2} + b^{2}} \right\} = e^{-at}\,\dfrac{1}{b}\sin(bt)\) applies with \(a = \tfrac12\) and \(b = \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x(t) \amp = e^{-t/2} \cdot \frac{1}{\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}} \sin\!\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) \\ \amp = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\,e^{-t/2} \sin\!\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right). \end{align*}
    This satisfies the ODE in the distributional sense and obeys the initial conditions exactly.
    🔗
    🔗

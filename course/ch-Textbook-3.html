    Section Overview

    This section shows how certain first-order differential equations can be solved simply by antidifferentiating. When the equation has the form \(y' = f(x)\text{,}\) its general solution is just an antiderivative of \(f(x)\) plus a constant. The text also clarifies the difference between definite and indefinite integrals and explains how initial conditions lead to a definiteâ€‘integral formula for the solution.

    • Correct. Differentiating gives \(y' = 3x^2\text{.}\) Any constant works.

    • Noâ€”its derivative is constant, not \(3x^2\text{.}\)

    3. Meaning of the Constant C.

    ðŸ”—

    4. Initial Value Problem.

    7. Finding a Solution Using a Definite Integral.
    Consider the initial value problem \(y' = \sin(x) + 2, y(0) = 5.\) Use the definite-integral formula to write the solution directly as an integral and then simplify your answer.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    If \(y' = f(x)\) and \(y(x_0) = y_0\text{,}\) then the solution is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = \int_{x_0}^{\,x} f(t)\,dt + y_0. \end{equation*}
    Here \(f(x) = \sin(x) + 2\text{,}\) \(x_0 = 0\text{,}\) and \(y_0 = 5\text{.}\) Therefore,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = \int_{0}^{\,x} \bigl(\sin(t) + 2\bigr)\,dt + 5. \end{equation*}
    Compute the integral:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{\,x} \sin(t)\,dt = 1 - \cos(x), \int_{0}^{\,x} 2\,dt = 2x. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = \bigl(1 - \cos(x)\bigr) + 2x + 5 = 6 - \cos(x) + 2x. \end{equation*}
    This solution matches the initial value and satisfies \(y'=\sin(x)+2\text{.}\)

    9.
    Write a differential equation that is a model for the following: In a city with a fixed population of \(P\) persons, the time rate of change of the number \(N\) of those persons infected with a certain contagious disease is proportional to the product of the number who have the disease and the number who do not.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The number infected is \(N\text{,}\) and the number not infected is \(P - N\text{.}\) If the rate is proportional to the product of these two quantities, then
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dN}{dt} = k\,N\,(P - N), \end{equation*}
    where \(k\) is a positive proportionality constant. This is the standard logistic-type infection model.

    10.
    Substitute \(y = e^{rx}\) into the differential equation \(y'' + y' - 2y = 0\) to determine all values of the constant \(r\) for which \(y = e^{rx}\) is a solution.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Compute derivatives:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{gather*} y = e^{rx}, \\ y' = r e^{rx}, \\ y'' = r^{2} e^{rx}. \end{gather*}
    Substitute into the equation:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} r^{2} e^{rx} + r e^{rx} - 2 e^{rx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Factor out \(e^{rx}\) (never zero):
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} r^{2} + r - 2 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solve the quadratic:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} (r + 2)(r - 1) = 0, \end{equation*}
    so the values are \(r = 1\) and \(r = -2\text{.}\)

    ðŸ”—

      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. Approximately how many years will it take for the salary to be 10 million?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Approximately how many years will it take for the salary to be 20 million?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since the salary grows by \(10\%\) each year, the exponential model is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} s(t) = 1\cdot (1.10)^{t}. \end{equation*}
